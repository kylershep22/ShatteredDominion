# THE SHATTERED DOMINION — RACE & ORIGIN SYSTEM
## Document 2 of 10 | Version 1.0 | Depends on: World Bible & Lore Foundation

**Purpose:** This document defines the three playable races and six origins available at launch. It covers physical characteristics, mechanical identity, origin backstories, starting conditions, NPC reaction frameworks, visual design direction, and expansion-ready hooks for future races. Every other document that references player race or origin defers to this one.

**Scope at Launch:** 3 races, 2 origins each (6 total starting experiences)
**Expansion-Ready:** Ironkin, Drakelings (designed in brief at end of document)

---

# SECTION 1: DESIGN PHILOSOPHY

## 1.1 Core Principles

**Identity without restriction.** Race and origin shape who you are in the world — how NPCs react to you, which doors open and close, what dialogue options you have — but they do not lock you into a playstyle. Any race can pursue any build. A Human can become a powerful mage. An Aetherborn can fight with a greatsword. The mechanical bonuses create tendencies, not walls.

**Social consequences are real.** Race is not cosmetic. An Aetherborn walking into Hollowford has a fundamentally different experience than a Human. Merchants may refuse service. Guards may detain you. NPCs may react with fear, hostility, curiosity, or pity. These reactions are consistent and logical within the world — they are not random prejudice for its own sake. They reflect the specific history and circumstances established in the World Bible.

**Origin is backstory with teeth.** Your origin determines where you were when the Severance hit, what you lost, what skills you carry, and how specific NPCs respond to your history. It provides 1-3 exclusive side quests, 40-60 unique dialogue lines across the game, and starting gear that reflects your former life. It does not determine your future — only your starting point.

**Start with nothing, earn everything.** Regardless of race or origin, every player begins the game wounded, poorly equipped, and socially marginal. A Frontier Settler lost their homestead. A Relic Hunter is blamed for the catastrophe. An Aetherborn literally didn't exist a few weeks ago. Progression should feel earned because the starting point is genuinely low.

## 1.2 Mechanical Balance Framework

Each race has:
- 3-5 meaningful **strengths** (mechanical advantages that shape playstyle tendencies)
- 2-3 meaningful **weaknesses** (manageable challenges that create texture, not punishment)
- 1 **unique system** (a race-specific mechanic that no other race has access to)

Weaknesses are designed as **puzzles, not penalties.** They create situations where the player must think differently — not situations where the player is arbitrarily weaker. Mid-game gear, skills, and knowledge should mitigate most racial weaknesses for players who invest in doing so.

Each origin has:
- +2 to one stat, +1 to another (stat bonuses are meaningful but not build-defining)
- 1-2 **passive abilities** (reflect background skills)
- **Starting gear** (thematically appropriate, mechanically functional for hours 1-5)
- **Social modifiers** (NPC disposition shifts based on origin recognition)
- 1-3 **exclusive side quests** (optional, not required for main story)
- 40-60 **unique dialogue lines** spread across the game

# SECTION 2: HUMANS — THE SCATTERED INHERITORS

*"We inherit everything. We deserve nothing."*

## 2.1 Lore Identity

Humans are the dominant population of the former Severant Dominion. They built their empire by plundering Firstborn technology they didn't understand, and now that technology is failing. Every faction in the current crisis is either led by humans or reacting to human decisions. Humans are everywhere — which means they're trusted nowhere specific. Every faction has reason to suspect human motives.

Humans are the race most directly responsible for the lattice's premature failure. The Dominion was a human empire. The exploitation was human exploitation. The three rituals that triggered the Severance were all performed by humans. This isn't a secret — NPCs of other races will reference it, sometimes bitterly.

But humans are also the most adaptable, the most politically fluent, and the most likely to survive the transition regardless of outcome. They've done it before. They'll do it again. That pragmatic resilience is their core identity.

**Population in the Vale:** Approximately 55% of the Severant Vale population is human — native settlers, refugees from other regions, and faction operatives.

## 2.2 Physical Characteristics

Humans in The Shattered Dominion look like real humans. No exaggeration, no stylization beyond what serves readability at game-camera distance.

**Body Structure:**
- Height: 1.6m–1.9m (full realistic range)
- Build: Diverse (mesomorphic, ectomorphic, endomorphic — varied by origin and lifestyle)
- Sexual dimorphism: Realistic human range
- No "heroic proportions" — characters look like people who work, fight, starve, and survive

**Skin:** Full human spectrum. All ethnicities represented. Skin shows weathering, scarring, sun damage, dirt, and age appropriate to origin and lifestyle. Clean skin is rare — this is a post-catastrophe world.

**Relic Mark:** Where the failsafe relic bonded (player choice during character creation: chest, forearm, or neck). Glowing runic pattern that pulses faintly. Visible to those who know what to look for. Can be partially concealed with clothing.

**Visual Design Note:** Humans are the baseline. Every other race's visual distinctiveness is measured against how different they look from a human at a glance. Humans should look grounded, practical, and real. Their clothing and gear tell their story — not their bodies.

## 2.3 Mechanical Identity

**Strengths:**
- **Siege Survivor:** +10% damage and defense when outnumbered (facing 2+ enemies simultaneously). Reflects the human experience of always being in over their heads and adapting.
- **Resourceful:** Equip gear up to 2 levels above normal requirement. Bonus consumables found in containers (food, potions, crafting scraps). Humans are scavengers by necessity.
- **Political Fluency:** Access to exclusive dialogue options — deception, intimidation, persuasion, and negotiation branches unavailable to other races. Reputation gains and losses are amplified by 25% (humans are political creatures; their actions carry more social weight).
- **Relic Affinity:** Can jury-rig damaged relics for temporary use (risk/reward mechanic — unstable relics may backfire). Better loot quality in Firstborn ruins (the Dominion's legacy of ruin-plundering gives humans an intuitive advantage in navigating these spaces). Can detect ambushes earlier (survival instinct honed by centuries of frontier life).

**Weaknesses:**
- **Arcane Tax:** +10% mana cost on all spells. Slightly longer cast times for advanced (Tier 3+) spells. Humans are not naturally attuned to magic — they can absolutely become powerful mages, but it requires more investment than it does for Vaelari or Aetherborn.
- **Faction Volatility:** The 25% reputation amplifier cuts both ways. Betrayals, failures, and controversial choices hit harder with human characters. NPCs expect more from humans politically and judge them more harshly for missteps.

**Unique System — Relic Jury-Rigging:**
Humans can interact with damaged or unstable Firstborn relics that other races cannot safely touch. When finding a damaged relic in the world, humans get the option to jury-rig it — a skill-check-style interaction where success grants a powerful temporary buff, consumable, or one-use ability, and failure causes damage, a debuff, or (rarely) an environmental hazard. This system rewards exploration and risk-taking, and it scales with the player's Intelligence and Perception stats.

**Playstyle Tendencies:** Hybrid builds, weapon specialists, diplomats, rogues. Humans can excel at magic with investment but naturally lean toward physical combat and social navigation. The recommended starting race for new players.

## 2.4 Origin A: Frontier Settler

*"I built something with my hands. Now it's ash."*

### Backstory

You've lived your whole life on the edges of the Severant Vale — farming, hunting, trapping, trading with passing caravans and distrusting the Dominion's officials who came to collect taxes on what you'd built yourself. You weren't political. You weren't ambitious. You just wanted to live.

The Severance destroyed your homestead. The sky tore open. Your livestock mutated — you had to kill animals you'd raised from birth. A lattice surge set your home on fire. A Firstborn cairn you'd used as a property marker for twenty years exploded, and a relic shard embedded in your body.

You woke in the wreckage. Everything you'd built was gone. The voice in your head told you to walk to Hollowford. You walked.

### Opening Sequence

The player's opening is set on the homestead during the Severance. Tutorial covers:
- Basic combat (fight mutated livestock with a hand axe)
- Dodging/blocking (a mutated bull charges — dodge or die)
- Scavenging (loot your own ruined home for supplies)
- Relic introduction (the Echo activates, teaches a basic spell)
- Environmental navigation (cross the damaged landscape toward Hollowford)
- Emotional beat: you find something personal in the wreckage — a family item, a keepsake. You can take it or leave it. This item has no mechanical value. It exists to make the loss feel real.

The tutorial ends when you reach the Hollowford road and see the walls in the distance. You're wounded, carrying salvage from your own life, and walking toward a town that doesn't know you're coming.

### Starting Stats & Gear

**Stats:** +2 Survival, +1 Endurance

**Passive Abilities:**
- **Tracker:** Highlight animal tracks and creature trails in the environment. Identify creature type and direction of travel. Functions as an exploration aid and early-warning system.
- **Wilderness Crafting:** Can craft basic consumables (bandages, simple poisons, torches, snares) from gathered materials without a crafting station. Recipes expand as the player discovers new materials.

**Starting Gear:**
- Hunting bow (ranged weapon, moderate damage, reliable)
- Hand axe (melee weapon, fast, doubles as a tool)
- Worn leather vest (light armor, minimal protection, practical)
- Waterskin, dried rations (3 days), basic herb pouch
- Flint and steel
- **Keepsake** (if taken during opening): a non-mechanical item that can be displayed in the player stronghold later. Small narrative payoff.

### Social Modifiers

**Positive Reactions:**
- Other frontier settlers recognize your mannerisms, accent, and practical knowledge. They trust you instinctively — you're "one of us."
- Vanguard soldiers respect self-sufficiency. Frontier settlers are seen as reliable, if unsophisticated.
- Merchants in rural areas offer fair prices. You know the value of goods.

**Negative Reactions:**
- Urban NPCs (Hollowford's established residents, nobles, scholars) dismiss you as a country rube. Some are condescending; others simply disinterested.
- Hollow Crown agents see no political value in you. You're invisible to their power calculations.
- Some Vaelari NPCs resent human settlers who built on Firstborn-regulated land without understanding (or caring about) the ecological cost.

**Unique Dialogue Examples:**
- "I know this soil. It's sick. The rain's wrong." (Environmental observation)
- "I didn't survive the Severance to be talked down to by a man in a clean shirt." (Pushing back on condescension)
- "You can't eat gold. Show me something useful." (Pragmatic negotiation)
- "I had a life before all this. Don't pretend you understand what I lost." (Emotional weight)

### Exclusive Side Quests (1-2)

**"Ashes of Home"**
Return to the ruins of your homestead after gaining enough strength (mid Act I). Discover that other frontier settlers have been displaced to a makeshift camp nearby. They're being harassed by mutated wildlife and pressured by a Vanguard patrol that wants to "relocate" them to a controlled settlement. Player choice: help the settlers defend their ground, broker a deal with the Vanguard, or convince the settlers to move (each with different consequences for the camp's future and your reputation).

**"The Old Cairn"**
The Firstborn cairn that marked your property is now an active relic site. Return to investigate. Discover it was a minor lattice monitoring station — and it recorded data about the Severance. Extracting the data requires relic interaction (tutorial for more advanced relic mechanics). The data is valuable to multiple factions. Who you give it to (or whether you keep it) affects your faction standing and provides lore context about the lattice failure's local effects.

## 2.5 Origin B: Relic Hunter

*"The Firstborn left answers. I intend to find them."*

### Backstory

You've spent years exploring Firstborn ruins. While the Dominion's nobility hoarded relics as status symbols and the Vanguard reverse-engineered them into crude weapons, you actually studied them. You learned to read Firstborn inscriptions (partially). You mapped ruin layouts. You catalogued relic types and theorized about their functions.

Some people call you a grave robber. Some call you a scholar. A growing number call you complicit in the Severance — the theory that relic hunters destabilized the lattice through reckless excavation isn't entirely wrong, and you know it. You weren't personally responsible, but your profession contributed to the conditions that made the catastrophe possible.

You were inside a Firstborn ruin when the Severance hit. The ruin came alive. Systems that had been dormant for millennia activated. A relic shard fused to your arm during the chaos. You barely escaped.

### Opening Sequence

The player's opening is set inside an active Firstborn ruin during the Severance. Tutorial covers:
- Basic combat (fight newly-activated construct drones)
- Environmental puzzles (navigate shifting corridors, avoid reactivated traps)
- Relic interaction (use the Echo to interface with a Firstborn control panel — teaches the relic mechanics in a context that feels natural for this origin)
- Stealth option (some constructs can be avoided by understanding their patrol patterns — the player's relic knowledge makes this intuitive)
- Lore discovery (find a Firstborn inscription that provides the first hint of the lattice's designed lifespan — a mystery hook that pays off in Act II)

The tutorial ends when the player escapes the ruin into daylight and sees the aftermath of the Severance — the changed sky, the distant smoke from Hollowford, the warped landscape. The contrast between the ruin's ordered interior and the chaos outside is the emotional beat.

### Starting Stats & Gear

**Stats:** +2 Perception, +1 Intelligence

**Passive Abilities:**
- **Relic Sense:** Detect Firstborn artifacts, hidden ruin entrances, and active lattice conduits through walls and obstacles within a 20m radius. Displayed as a subtle visual pulse in the environment. The range and clarity improve as the player levels Perception.
- **Ancient Linguist:** Can read Firstborn inscriptions without the training that other origins require. Inscriptions appear translated in the UI. This opens lore entries, puzzle solutions, and hidden room locations that other origins must find alternative routes to access.

**Starting Gear:**
- Scholar's staff (medium weapon, moderate damage, can be used as a focus for spell amplification)
- Weathered explorer's coat (light armor, many pockets, modest protection)
- Relic scanner (tool item — pulses and glows when near Firstborn technology, complementing the Relic Sense ability with a physical prop)
- Field journal (fills with lore entries as the player discovers relics and inscriptions — readable in the inventory, functions as a personal codex)
- 2 unstable relics (consumable items — powerful one-use effects with a failure chance. Demonstrate the Human jury-rigging system immediately)
- Small coin purse (less starting gold than other origins — relic hunters aren't wealthy)

### Social Modifiers

**Positive Reactions:**
- Scholars, mages, and Verdant Flame researchers respect your knowledge. They treat you as a colleague. Access to academic-adjacent questlines and information trading.
- Ironkin NPCs (when encountered) are curious — you understand forge mechanisms and Firstborn metalwork better than most humans. Cautious respect.
- Other relic hunters offer partnership opportunities and share leads on unexplored sites.
- Aetherborn NPCs feel a strange kinship — you both seek understanding of the Firstborn's legacy.

**Negative Reactions:**
- Traditionalist Vaelari blame relic hunters specifically for lattice destabilization. Some are hostile. Others are coldly dismissive.
- Superstitious commoners think relic hunters caused the Severance. You may be verbally accosted, refused service, or reported to authorities.
- Vanguard patrols may detain you for "unauthorized excavation" — your profession is technically illegal under martial law. Your Relic Hunter identity can be concealed in some interactions.
- Some Hollow Crown agents see you as a threat — you know things about Firstborn technology that undermine their narrative of divine inheritance.

**Unique Dialogue Examples:**
- "I've seen this glyph before. It's a warning marker — maintenance access, not a tomb." (Correcting lore misconceptions)
- "This relic is Firstborn, Third Era — and it's still active. Don't touch it." (Technical expertise)
- "I didn't cause the Severance. But I know who did, and I know why the lattice let it happen." (Quest hook dialogue)
- "Everyone plundered these ruins. I'm the one who actually read the writing on the walls." (Defending your profession)

### Exclusive Side Quests (1-2)

**"The First Sin"**
A faction (Vanguard or Verdant Flame, depending on the player's current reputation balance) formally accuses you of contributing to the Severance through reckless excavation. You must either prove your specific innocence (investigate the ruin you were in during the Severance — it holds evidence that the site was already compromised by Dominion mining before you arrived), embrace the accusation strategically (accepting guilt in exchange for leverage — "I know more about what went wrong than anyone, and you need me"), or discredit the accuser (dig up evidence of their own complicity in lattice exploitation). Each path has distinct narrative and reputation consequences.

**"The Cartographer's Legacy"**
You learn of a legendary relic hunter — Oren Ashwell — who mapped an entire Firstborn transit network before the Severance. His maps would reveal hidden ruin entrances, sealed vaults, and potentially a direct route toward the Heart of the Lattice. Finding his workshop (hidden, trapped, and partially collapsed) requires navigating environmental puzzles and relic interactions. The maps themselves become a gameplay reward: unlocking new dungeon locations on the player's world map that other origins must discover through exploration or NPC tips.

# SECTION 3: VAELARI — THE UNWILLING AWOKEN

*"We slept. We should have stayed sleeping."*

## 3.1 Lore Identity

The Vaelari are not elves — but that's the closest reference point most humans have. They are a distinct species: tall, long-lived, and deeply attuned to the natural world and the Wellspring, the continent's largest natural magic nexus. Their connection to nature is spiritual and magical, not biological — they are not plant creatures or tree-spirits. They are people, with culture, politics, and individual identity, who happen to have an innate bond with the living world that humans lack.

Vaelari life cycles are different from human life. They enter chrysalis states — long periods of magical dormancy in specially prepared chambers — during which their bodies regenerate, their minds process centuries of accumulated experience, and their connection to the Wellspring deepens. The chrysalis is not a cocoon or a seed pod — it is a ritual construct of woven enchantment, resembling a translucent shell of solidified light. A single Vaelari may live for millennia, cycling between active periods of decades or centuries and chrysalis periods of similar duration.

The Severance changed everything. The Wellspring, connected to a major lattice anchor, destabilized violently. The psychic shockwave shattered thousands of chrysalises simultaneously — waking Vaelari who weren't ready, damaging others who emerged incomplete, and flooding every living Vaelari with the Wellspring's agony.

The Vaelari are now a people in existential crisis. Their life cycle is broken. Their sacred site is in pain. Their internal factions are at war over how to respond. And the wider world — which mostly ignored them before — now alternately fears them, pities them, and blames them.

**Population in the Vale:** Approximately 12% of the Vale population. Mostly refugees (Newly Awakened who fled Verdantheart's chaos, Driftless who were already living among humans). They are visibly alien, socially marginal, and caught between two worlds.

## 3.2 Physical Characteristics

Vaelari should look hauntingly beautiful — tall, graceful, and slightly otherworldly, but fundamentally humanoid. Think Tolkien's elves filtered through Witcher 3's grounded aesthetic: recognizably person-shaped, undeniably not human, and unsettling in the way that something almost-familiar-but-not-quite always is. Their connection to nature is spiritual and magical, not biological — they are not plant creatures.

**Body Structure:**
- Height: 1.9m–2.2m (taller than humans, noticeably so)
- Build: Ectomorphic (lean, long-limbed, graceful). Limbs slightly elongated compared to human proportions — not dramatically, but enough to register subconsciously. Fingers slender and long. Neck slightly elongated.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal (androgynous by human standards, though individual variation exists)
- Weight: Lighter than appearance suggests (lower bone density, leaner muscle)
- Movement: Fluid, deliberate, efficient — no wasted motion. Where a human shifts weight, fidgets, and adjusts, a Vaelari is still until they move, and then the movement is precise. This reads as elegant to some observers and predatory to others.

**Skin:**
- Texture: Smooth, pale, with an almost luminous quality — as if faintly lit from within, especially in low light. Not glowing like an Aetherborn — more like moonlight on pale stone.
- Color range: Pale spectrum with cool undertones — alabaster, ivory, light ash, cool olive, dusky gray. Distinctly paler and cooler-toned than most humans, but recognizably skin, not bark or plant matter.
- Subtle tells: Faint vein-like patterns visible on the temples, wrists, and neck — not glowing, but slightly darker than surrounding skin, resembling natural veining in marble. These darken and become more prominent when the Vaelari is in pain, using magic, or near the Wellspring. Older Vaelari have more pronounced patterns.
- Weathering: Vaelari skin doesn't age the way human skin does — no wrinkles, no sun damage. Instead, older Vaelari develop a quality like weathered stone — still smooth, but with a visible weight of time. This makes it difficult for humans to accurately guess a Vaelari's age.

**Ears:**
- Elongated and tapered — the most immediately visible non-human feature besides height. Not exaggerated (not 15cm fantasy-elf ears), but clearly pointed and approximately 2-3cm longer than human ears. They contribute to the angular, sharp-featured silhouette.

**Eyes:**
- Color: Unusual spectrum — pale green, silver-green, amber, ice blue, or violet. Colors that exist in nature but rarely in human eyes. No brown or dark eyes.
- Glow: A faint luminescence that intensifies with emotion, magic use, or Wellspring proximity. Subtle in daylight (barely perceptible), visible in shadow or darkness. Not a lantern — more like light catching in a cat's eye.
- Pupils: Present but vertically elongated (slightly — not slit-pupiled like a reptile, but enough to notice at close range). This creates a subtly alien quality during direct eye contact.

**Hair:**
- Real hair — not foliage, not vines, not plant matter. It grows, it can be cut, it moves with wind and gravity like human hair. However, it has qualities that mark it as distinctly Vaelari:
- Color: Unusual natural tones — silver-white, ash blonde, deep auburn, blue-black, or (rarely) a faded green-gray. Colors that a human could theoretically have but rarely does. No bright or saturated colors. Post-Severance, many Vaelari's hair has dulled or silvered prematurely — a visible symptom of Wellspring distress.
- Texture: Fine, straight or gently waved. Has a faint sheen that catches light differently than human hair — almost metallic in quality.
- Styles: Practical and cultural. Long hair is traditional (often braided, tied back, or pinned with bone or wood clasps). Shorter styles exist, especially among Driftless Vaelari who've adopted human grooming norms. No elaborate fantasy styling — grounded, functional, real.

**Facial Features:**
- Angular. High cheekbones, narrow jaw, straight or slightly aquiline nose. Features that read as sharp and defined — striking rather than soft. Not identical across all Vaelari (there is individual variation), but the overall impression is of faces carved rather than grown.
- Expression: Subtler than human expression. Vaelari communicate emotion through eyes and voice more than through broad facial movement. This makes them difficult for humans to read, which contributes to social friction — humans often perceive Vaelari as cold, aloof, or deceptive when the Vaelari is simply expressing normally.

**Relic Mark:** Same as humans — player choice of location (chest, forearm, neck). The mark's runic pattern has a slightly more organic quality — flowing lines rather than sharp geometric angles — but is recognizably Firstborn in origin. Pulses with a green-gold light rather than the blue-white of humans.

**Visual Design Note:** The "Five at a Glance" test — in silhouette, a Vaelari should be immediately distinguishable from a Human by height, proportions (elongated limbs, pointed ears), and stillness of posture, even without color or detail. They should look like they belong in the same world as humans — not like they wandered in from a different art style.

## 3.3 Mechanical Identity

**Strengths:**
- **Nature Bond:** Passive HP regeneration when in natural environments (forests, fields, waterways — not towns, not dungeons, not indoors). Rate scales with time of day (faster during daylight hours — the Wellspring's energy flows more strongly through natural spaces under sunlight). Animals in natural environments are non-hostile unless provoked.
- **Wellspring Sense:** Detect corruption zones, active magic sources, and lattice nodes at extended range (40m, versus the standard 10m awareness radius). Corruption appears as visible dark veins in the environment; magic sources glow. This is an exploration advantage that makes Vaelari excellent at finding hidden content.
- **Greenway Travel:** +15% movement speed on natural terrain (grass, forest floor, riverbanks). This stacks with mounts. In dense forest specifically, Vaelari can navigate root-paths and canopy routes that other races cannot access — alternative movement options in Verdantheart expansion content.
- **Communion:** Can interact with Wellspring shrine locations (scattered throughout the Vale, more common near Verdantheart border) for temporary buffs, fast-travel options, and lore visions. Other races can visit these shrines but cannot activate them.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Urban Discomfort:** Nature Bond regeneration is disabled in settlements, indoors, and in stone/metal environments. The Vaelari body is designed for natural spaces. Cities feel wrong. This doesn't make urban questing impossible — it removes a passive advantage and creates mild resource pressure (more reliance on consumables and healing items while in town).
- **Lattice Pain:** Proximity to unstable lattice nodes causes periodic damage ticks and brief disorientation (screen distortion, movement slowdown lasting 1-2 seconds). This is the Wellspring connection translating lattice instability into physical pain. Manageable with specific gear and skills, but it makes certain dungeon segments more challenging for Vaelari.
- **Social Alienation:** Most human NPCs in the Vale react to Vaelari with a spectrum from unease to open hostility. This manifests as: higher prices at some merchants (10-25% markup), restricted access to certain locations (private residences, some faction areas), hostile random encounters (drunk xenophobes, scared villagers calling for guards), and fewer available persuasion dialogue options in early-game interactions (before the player has built reputation). This softens significantly as reputation grows.

**Unique System — Wellspring Communion:**
At Wellspring shrine locations (approximately 8-10 in the Vale), Vaelari can enter a communion state. This provides:
- A temporary buff (duration: 30 minutes game time). Buff type varies by shrine — combat buff, exploration buff, magic buff, or resistance buff.
- A lore vision — a brief environmental memory showing what the shrine's location looked like before the Severance. These are worldbuilding moments, not gameplay-critical, but they enrich the narrative and occasionally provide hints about nearby hidden content.
- A fast-travel anchor. Communion shrines become fast-travel points for Vaelari characters once activated. (Other races must discover and activate separate fast-travel points — Firstborn waystone markers.)

**Playstyle Tendencies:** Druid builds, ranger builds, control mages, nature summoners. Vaelari naturally excel at magic and exploration. Their weaknesses push them out of heavy urban/dungeon gameplay loops, creating a playstyle that alternates between natural environments (where they're powerful) and hostile environments (where they must compensate). Moderate difficulty — recommended for players comfortable with resource management.

## 3.4 Origin A: Newly Awakened

*"I don't understand this world. But I will."*

### Backstory

You emerged from your chrysalis days — possibly only hours — before the game begins. You have no context for anything. The Severance shattered your chrysalis prematurely, and you fell into a world of chaos, noise, and pain. Other Vaelari around you were screaming, confused, or already dead (chrysalis rupture is not always survivable). The Wellspring's agony flooded your newborn consciousness like a wall of sound.

You don't know what the Dominion was. You don't know what the Severance is. You don't know what humans are. You learn everything from scratch — and you learn fast, because the alternative is death.

A relic shard, carried on the Wellspring's shockwave, struck you during the chaos. The Echo's voice was the first coherent thing you heard.

### Opening Sequence

The player wakes inside a shattered chrysalis, falling to the ground of a Verdantheart grove in crisis. Tutorial covers:
- Movement basics (learning to walk — the player character's first steps are literally their first steps, with a brief stumbling animation that resolves into normal movement within seconds)
- Nature magic basics (instinctive magic — the player reaches for a falling Vaelari and accidentally casts a binding spell, arcane energy rooting the other Vaelari's feet to the ground and stabilizing them. The magic feels like breathing, not like a learned skill)
- Environmental danger (corruption is visible in the grove — black veins in the soil, wilting canopy, dying chrysalises. One corrupted animal attacks — combat tutorial)
- Relic introduction (the Echo activates, providing direction and basic survival instruction in a voice the Newly Awakened doesn't understand but instinctively follows)
- Emotional beat: another Newly Awakened — confused, terrified, stumbling — looks to you for help. You can guide them toward safety (they survive and become a minor NPC in the Vale later) or focus on your own escape (they are not seen again). No mechanical consequence. Establishes the player's relationship with their own vulnerability.

The tutorial ends at the border of Verdantheart, looking out into the Vale — alien, open, and nothing like the grove. A signpost in a language you can't read points toward Hollowford. The Echo translates: *"Civilization. Seek it. You need... others."*

### Starting Stats & Gear

**Stats:** +2 Adaptability, +1 Willpower

**Passive Abilities:**
- **Accelerated Learning:** +15% XP bonus for the first time the player performs any action category (first kill with a new weapon type, first successful persuasion, first crafting attempt, first relic interaction, etc.). Reflects the Newly Awakened's sponge-like capacity for new experience. Diminishes as the player levels — the XP bonus drops to +10% at level 10, +5% at level 20, and disappears at level 30.
- **Innocent Eye:** Certain NPCs who would normally be hostile or dismissive soften toward a Newly Awakened Vaelari. You are visibly naive and non-threatening. This provides access to some dialogue options that other origins (including other Vaelari origins) don't get — people are willing to explain things to you that they'd never explain to someone they perceived as a peer.

**Starting Gear:**
- Chrysalis wraps (light armor — remnants of the enchanted chrysalis shell, hardened into a flexible, semi-translucent protective layer. Faintly luminous. Distinctive, otherworldly appearance.)
- Wooden staff (melee weapon — a branch torn from the grove during the escape. Unrefined, functional.)
- Hearthstone shard (a small Wellspring-attuned crystal — can be used 5 times to create a brief restorative aura in natural environments, healing the player and nearby allies over 10 seconds. Recharges at Wellspring shrines. An early introduction to the Vaelari's nature-magic resource loop.)
- No gold. No tools. No human-readable items. You own literally nothing from the human world.

### Social Modifiers

**Positive Reactions:**
- Elder Vaelari (NPC encounters) feel protective. They offer guidance, context, and occasionally gear. Your innocence triggers their caretaker instincts.
- Aelira Wynnroot specifically responds to Newly Awakened Vaelari with heightened guilt and investment — she feels personally responsible for your premature awakening. This creates a stronger initial bond with her companion arc.
- Some human NPCs in Hollowford respond with genuine sympathy. You're clearly lost, clearly vulnerable, and clearly not a threat. A few will offer small acts of kindness (a meal, directions, a warning about dangerous areas).

**Negative Reactions:**
- Most human NPCs see an alien creature that doesn't speak their language fluently and react with fear or suspicion. The sympathy window is small — if you don't build reputation quickly, it closes.
- Driftless Vaelari (who severed their Wellspring connection voluntarily) view Newly Awakened as a reminder of what they gave up. Some are resentful. Some are pitying. Few are neutral.
- Vanguard patrols flag Vaelari as potential security threats — you're unknown, you have magic, and you don't follow human social norms. Expect to be stopped, questioned, and occasionally detained.

**Unique Dialogue Examples:**
- "What is... money? You exchange metal for food? Why not just... grow the food?" (Genuine confusion)
- "I have been alive for twelve days. Do not tell me what I cannot do." (Defiance)
- "The Wellspring screams. Can you not hear it? How do you bear the silence?" (Alien perspective)
- "I am learning your words. Please speak slower." (Early game — frequency decreases as the player progresses, reflecting the character's rapid adaptation)

### Exclusive Side Quests

**"First Steps"**
In mid-Act I, you encounter 3-4 other Newly Awakened Vaelari who made it to the Vale but are struggling to survive. They look to you — the one with the relic mark, the one who seems to understand this world better than they do — for guidance. The quest involves finding them shelter, food, and safety. The choices you make determine whether they integrate into Hollowford's refugee population (safe but culturally painful), return to Verdantheart's border (dangerous but familiar), or form an independent enclave (fragile but self-determined). Their fate becomes a recurring thread — you hear about them throughout the game.

## 3.5 Origin B: Voluntary Driftless

*"I chose to leave. I didn't choose this."*

### Backstory

Years ago — possibly decades — you made the most controversial decision a Vaelari can make: you left Verdantheart voluntarily, severing your active connection to the Wellspring. You walked into the human world and began building a life there.

The reasons vary. Maybe the grove's politics suffocated you. Maybe you fell in love with a human. Maybe you were simply curious. Whatever the reason, you cut the cord — and your body began the slow process of declining. Without the Wellspring's sustaining energy, Vaelari biology degrades over decades. Your hair has silvered prematurely. The veining on your skin has darkened and spread. Your eyes are dimmer than a connected Vaelari's. You've accepted this as the price of freedom.

The Severance complicated everything. The moment the Wellspring destabilized, your severed connection screamed back to life — a single agonizing pulse that dropped you to your knees and left you with a relic shard embedded in your body. For the first time in years, you felt the Wellspring. It was in agony. And now it won't let go.

You were living in a border settlement — half-human, half-Vaelari trading post — when the Severance hit. The humans around you turned. Blame. Fear. You ran.

### Opening Sequence

The player is in a settlement when the Severance erupts. Tutorial covers:
- Social navigation (the tutorial's first challenge is not combat — it's escaping a hostile crowd. NPCs shout, throw things, block exits. The player learns movement and environmental awareness under social pressure, not physical danger.)
- Combat (a panicked human attacks with a knife. Basic melee tutorial. The player is forced to fight or disarm — killing the attacker has a minor reputation consequence if witnessed; disarming does not.)
- Stealth/evasion (optional — the player can sneak through back alleys instead of pushing through the crowd. Stealth mechanics introduced.)
- Relic introduction (the Echo activates during the Wellspring pulse — the pain and the voice arrive simultaneously. The player must use a relic ability to break through a collapsed wall and escape the settlement.)
- Emotional beat: your sympathizer — the human NPC who was your closest friend in the settlement — finds you before you escape. They press supplies into your hands and tell you to run. They can't come with you. Their fate is uncertain (resolved in a later side quest).

The tutorial ends on the road to Hollowford. You're alone, bleeding, carrying supplies from a friend who may already be dead, and for the first time in years, the Wellspring is screaming in your head.

### Starting Stats & Gear

**Stats:** +2 Independence, +1 Charisma

**Passive Abilities:**
- **Cultural Bridge:** Fluent in human language and social norms. No language barrier with human NPCs (unlike Newly Awakened). Access to human social spaces that other Vaelari are excluded from. Can pass for "mostly human" in initial interactions if wearing concealing clothing — though prolonged interaction or physical inspection reveals the truth.
- **Severed Resilience:** Immune to Wellspring-related pain effects that affect other Vaelari (the lattice pain weakness is reduced by 50%). However, also immune to Wellspring shrine buffs (the connection is damaged, not restored — the Severance's pulse was a momentary reconnection, not a permanent one). This creates a different mechanical profile from Newly Awakened: fewer highs, fewer lows, more consistent baseline.

**Starting Gear:**
- Short sword (melee weapon — a human-made blade you've carried for years. Well-maintained, practical.)
- Mixed clothing (human-style tunic, Vaelari-cut trousers, hooded cloak for concealment. The outfit tells a story of dual identity.)
- Trade goods (small pouch of valuable herbs and remedies — the kind of goods a border-settlement Vaelari would carry. Can be sold for starting gold or used as crafting materials.)
- Sympathizer's token (a carved wooden pendant given by the friend who helped you escape. Non-mechanical — but showing it to certain NPCs later triggers unique dialogue responses from people who knew your friend.)
- Moderate starting gold (you had a life and savings — more than the Newly Awakened, comparable to a human settler)

### Social Modifiers

**Positive Reactions:**
- Human NPCs who are accustomed to non-human individuals (merchants, tavern keepers, frontier types) treat you as a familiar presence. You're a known quantity — the Vaelari who acts human. This is comfortable for them.
- Reaver contacts recognize you from the border trade networks. Access to black market connections from the start.
- Aetherborn NPCs (when encountered) relate to your experience of existing between worlds.

**Negative Reactions:**
- Traditionalist Vaelari consider you a traitor to your species. The word "Driftless" is used as an insult. Some Vaelari NPCs refuse to speak with you. Aelira is conflicted — she respects your independence but grieves your choice.
- Humans who discover you're Vaelari (if you've been passing) may feel deceived. The trust penalty is sharper than the trust penalty for being openly Vaelari.
- Newly Awakened Vaelari are confused by you — you're what they might become, and some find that terrifying.
- Your premature silver hair, darkened veining, and dim eyes mark you as declining to any Vaelari who looks closely. Pity is common. Respect for the choice is not.

**Unique Dialogue Examples:**
- "I left Verdantheart because I wanted to choose my own life. I didn't expect the choice to follow me." (Personal philosophy)
- "I know how humans think. I've been thinking like one for years. That's not a compliment or an insult — it's a fact." (Cultural fluency)
- "The Wellspring is screaming again. I cut that cord. It grew back." (Post-Severance reckoning)
- "Don't call me Driftless. That's their word for what I am. I call it free." (Identity assertion)

### Exclusive Side Quests

**"The Bridge Burns"**
Your sympathizer from the border settlement is alive — but in danger. The settlement has devolved into anti-Vaelari violence, and your friend is suspected of harboring Vaelari sympathies (which they did — they harbored you). Return to the settlement (now hostile territory) to extract them. The quest involves stealth, social manipulation, or combat depending on approach. Success brings the sympathizer to Hollowford as an NPC ally. Failure means they're imprisoned or killed — and the player must live with that.

# SECTION 4: AETHERBORN — THE SEVERED CHILDREN

*"We were not born. We were torn."*

## 4.1 Lore Identity

Aetherborn are not a race in the biological sense. They are arcane anomalies — beings spontaneously generated by the Severance itself. When the lattice cascade tore through the continent, raw lattice energy discharged at fracture points — battlefields, relic sites, places of intense magical activity — and in a handful of cases, that energy coalesced into humanoid form.

They are living lattice constructs. Their bodies are made of solidified arcane energy shaped into something approximately human. They have skin, but it's translucent. They have blood, but it glows. They have minds, but they were assembled from lattice data — fragments of recorded thoughts, residual emotional imprints, and raw processing logic — rather than born from organic development.

Every Aetherborn is unique. They manifested under different conditions, absorbed different data fragments, and stabilized (or failed to stabilize) in different ways. But they share common traits: lattice dependency (their bodies degrade without access to lattice energy), extraordinary magical ability (they are made of what other races merely channel), and radical alienness (they are weeks old in bodies that look adult, thinking with minds assembled from other people's memories).

The world doesn't know what to make of them. The Vanguard wants to control or weaponize them. The Verdant Flame wants to study them. The Reavers want to exploit them. The Hollow Crown wants to understand if they can be replicated. And ordinary people — already terrified by the Severance — see walking, glowing proof that the world has fundamentally changed.

**Population in the Vale:** Extremely rare. Fewer than 50 Aetherborn are known to exist on the entire continent. Perhaps 5-8 are in the Vale region. The player is the most stable and long-surviving Aetherborn anyone has encountered.

## 4.2 Physical Characteristics

Aetherborn should look wrong. Not ugly — beautiful, in fact — but wrong. The uncanny valley, rendered in light.

**Body Structure:**
- Height: Variable (1.7m–2.0m — manifested at whatever height the lattice data suggested)
- Build: Lean, symmetrical, idealized — too perfect. No asymmetries, no imperfections, no blemishes. This is unsettling, not attractive, because real bodies aren't this uniform.
- Weight: Lighter than appearance suggests (partially energy, not fully physical)
- Movement: Feet barely touch the ground (1-2cm hover, subtle). Movement looks like gliding rather than walking. Running produces afterimage trails. Jumping involves extended hang-time and slow descent.

**Skin:**
- Translucent. Internal arcane veins visible — glowing lines of blue-white energy running beneath the surface like luminous blood vessels.
- Base tone: Pale, with a faint blue or lavender undertone. No human skin tones.
- Texture: Smooth — unnaturally so. Dirt, blood, and grime slide off. An Aetherborn in a muddy battlefield is still clean. This is conspicuous and creepy.
- Transparency: Increases when HP is low. At critical health, you can see through the Aetherborn's limbs. This is a visual health indicator and a lore-consistent expression of instability.

**Eyes:**
- No visible pupils. Solid color — luminous blue, white, lavender, or silver.
- Glow intensifies with emotion or magic use.
- Eye contact with an Aetherborn is described by human NPCs as "like looking at a lamp" — beautiful but you instinctively look away.

**Hair:**
- Appears to be hair but behaves like no physical substance. Semi-transparent at the tips. Floats as if underwater — responds to magic, not wind or gravity. Faintly luminous.
- Color: Always arcane tones — white, pale blue, lavender, silver. Never natural human colors.

**Arcane Manifestation (Defining Visual Feature):**
- Relic Mark: The Aetherborn's mark isn't where a relic bonded to them — the relic is part of what they are. The mark covers a larger area than other races (potentially torso-spanning) and pulses more visibly.
- Instability Events: Brief, random visual glitches where the Aetherborn's form distorts for 1-2 frames. Like a rendering error in reality. Frequency increases at low HP or high magic use.
- Afterimage: Quick movement leaves a trailing echo — an arcane afterimage that fades over 1-2 seconds.
- Phase Transparency: At low HP, the Aetherborn becomes partially transparent. Not invisible — translucent. You can see the environment through them. This is deeply unsettling to NPCs who witness it.

## 4.3 Mechanical Identity

**Strengths:**
- **Arcane Mastery:** Highest base magic damage of any race. Largest mana pool. Reduced spell cooldowns (15% faster). Spells have enhanced visual effects (not just cosmetic — some spells gain additional mechanics, like fire spells leaving lingering burn zones that persist 2 seconds longer than other races' versions).
- **Phase Step:** Replaces the standard dodge roll. A short-range teleport (5m range) that leaves an arcane afterimage at the origin point. The afterimage persists for 1 second and draws enemy aggro (enemies attack it briefly before retargeting). This is both a defensive and tactical tool.
- **Lattice Resonance:** Can interface directly with Firstborn technology without tools or training. Relic puzzles, sealed doors, lattice node interactions — all simplified for Aetherborn. The Echo communicates more clearly with Aetherborn hosts (earlier coherence in the narrative, additional dialogue lines).
- **Overload:** An active system. The player can push any spell beyond its safe output — spending HP instead of additional mana to amplify damage, area of effect, or duration by 50-100%. Visual effect: the Aetherborn's body visibly destabilizes (glitching, transparency increase, afterimages multiply). High risk, high reward. The skill ceiling for Aetherborn play is defined by Overload management.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Degradation:** Aetherborn HP decays passively over time — approximately 1% per 5 minutes of real time. This is arrested (paused, not reversed) near active lattice nodes, Firstborn ruins, and Wellspring shrines. It is reversed by specific consumables (lattice essence, arcane stabilizers — crafted or purchased). Early game, this creates mild resource pressure. Mid-to-late game, the player should have enough resources and knowledge to manage it comfortably. It never stops entirely — it is a permanent reminder of what the Aetherborn is.
- **Fragility:** Lowest base physical defense of any race. Lowest HP pool (offset by Phase Step and Overload). The Aetherborn is a glass cannon by default — builds that mitigate this are possible but require deliberate investment.
- **Hunted:** Multiple factions want Aetherborn for different reasons (study, weaponization, fear-based persecution). This manifests as: unique hostile random encounters (faction capture squads), restricted access to more locations than other races, and the highest baseline social hostility. Some merchants flatly refuse service. Some quest givers refuse to speak with you. Building reputation is slower and harder — but the payoff when NPCs come to accept you is more significant.

**Unique System — Overload:**
The Overload system is the Aetherborn's defining mechanic. Any spell can be Overloaded by holding the cast button beyond the normal release point. A visual indicator (the relic mark pulsing faster, the screen's edge distorting) shows the charge building. Release at the right moment for a controlled Overload (50% boost, manageable HP cost). Hold too long and the Overload becomes uncontrolled (100% boost but massive HP cost, random trajectory, potential self-damage, and a 5-second cooldown on all abilities).

Mastery of Overload timing is what separates adequate Aetherborn play from exceptional Aetherborn play. Certain items, runes, and Lattice Formulas interact with the Overload system — reducing HP cost, extending the controlled window, adding secondary effects on Overload, or converting Overload self-damage into a beneficial effect.

**Playstyle Tendencies:** Mage builds, spellblade builds, glass-cannon burst damage, tactical positioning. Aetherborn are the hardest race to play well and the most powerful when played optimally. Recommended for experienced players.

## 4.4 Origin A: Fracture Core

*"I was not born. I was detonated."*

### Backstory

You manifested at the epicenter of a major lattice fracture — a place where the Severance discharged an extraordinary amount of energy. You were not assembled gradually. You were instantaneous — a detonation of arcane force that resolved into a humanoid shape in approximately three seconds.

You have no predecessor memories. No absorbed personality fragments. No context. Your mind is raw lattice processing power shaped into something that can think, but it had to teach itself how to think. Your first thought was *light*. Your second thought was *pain*. Your third thought was *run*, because the Vanguard soldiers who witnessed your manifestation were already raising weapons.

You are the most powerful type of Aetherborn — and the most unstable. Your body is a barely-contained arcane reaction. Your relic mark isn't a bond between you and a Firstborn artifact — the artifact is your skeleton. You are the relic.

### Opening Sequence

The player experiences manifestation. This is disorienting by design — the most unconventional opening of the six.

Tutorial covers:
- **Existence basics:** The screen is white. Sound fades in. The player gains visual input gradually — shapes, then colors, then depth. Movement tutorial begins before the player can fully see (move toward sound, away from light intensity).
- **Body control:** First spell cast is involuntary — the player's body discharges energy, throwing nearby debris. This teaches the magic system as an instinctive process rather than a learned skill.
- **Threat response:** Vanguard soldiers attack. Combat tutorial — but framed as the player not understanding why these beings are hostile. The Echo activates and provides the first coherent thought: *"They fear you. Move. NOW."*
- **Phase Step introduction:** A soldier corners the player. Phase Step triggers instinctively — the player teleports 5m and sees their own afterimage standing where they were. The soldier attacks the afterimage. This is Phase Step's tutorial and an immediate demonstration of Aetherborn tactical potential.
- **Emotional beat:** During the escape, the player passes a reflective surface (puddle, polished metal, broken mirror) and sees themselves for the first time. A beat of stillness. The Echo: *"You are... new. This will be... difficult. I am sorry."*

Tutorial ends in the wilderness outside the fracture zone. The player is alone, doesn't understand language yet (the Echo translates initially — subtitles appear with a slight delay early on, normalizing over Act I), and must navigate toward Hollowford using the Echo's guidance and environmental cues.

### Starting Stats & Gear

**Stats:** +3 Arcane Power (unique — Fracture Core is the only origin with a +3 bonus, offset by having no second stat bonus and harsher starting conditions)

**Passive Abilities:**
- **Volatile Conduit:** Overload's controlled window is 20% wider (easier to hit the sweet spot), but uncontrolled Overload deals 25% more self-damage. Higher ceiling, lower floor.
- **Arcane Presence:** Passive magic-detection aura. Nearby magical items, hidden runes, and concealed lattice conduits glow faintly when the player is within 10m. Functions as an ambient exploration aid.

**Starting Gear:**
- Ethereal wraps (minimal armor — cloth bindings that shimmer with arcane energy. Barely protective, visually distinctive.)
- Stabilization amulet (neck slot — reduces HP degradation rate by 30%. Essential early-game survival item. Can be replaced by better stabilization gear later.)
- Nothing else. No weapon (the Fracture Core IS a weapon — their hands discharge energy). No gold. No food. No tools. You own nothing because you ARE nothing — you're three weeks old.

### Social Modifiers

**Positive Reactions:**
- Verdant Flame researchers are fascinated. You are the most direct evidence of what the Severance actually did to lattice energy. They want to study you — which is intrusive but not hostile. Access to Verdant Flame lore and support earlier than other origins.
- Other Aetherborn (the few you encounter) feel immediate kinship. No other being in the world understands what you are.
- A small subset of human NPCs see you as miraculous rather than monstrous — proof that the Severance created something new, not just destroyed. These NPCs become potential allies.

**Negative Reactions:**
- Most human NPCs are terrified. You glow. You float. You appeared out of a magical explosion. Every instinct says "danger." Expect hostility, avoidance, and occasional violence.
- Vanguard forces consider you a security threat by default. Detainment attempts are more aggressive and more frequent than for other races.
- Hollow Crown agents see you as either a tool to be claimed or a threat to be eliminated. Seraphine is the exception — she's interested, not afraid, which is its own kind of danger.
- Reavers want to know if you can be replicated. Their interest is mercenary. Some will try to capture you for study or sale.

**Unique Dialogue Examples:**
- "I have been alive for twenty-three days. I have been attacked seventeen times. Your world is... aggressive." (Early game observation)
- "You call me abomination. I call you slow. We both have perspective problems." (Pushing back)
- "I am made of the thing that broke your world. I understand if that frightens you. It frightens me too." (Vulnerability)
- "The lattice is not just beneath the ground. It's beneath my skin. I hear it all the time." (Unique perspective on the central conflict)

### Exclusive Side Quests

**"Ground Zero"**
Return to the fracture point where you manifested (mid Act II, once strong enough). The site is still active — a permanent corruption zone of unusual properties (the energy here is creative, not purely destructive; strange flora and fauna are emerging). Investigating the site reveals data about what the lattice was trying to do when it discharged — was your creation accidental, or was the lattice trying to build something? The answer is ambiguous by design, but the data is valuable to every faction. Who you share it with has significant reputation consequences.

## 4.5 Origin B: Settlement Emergent

*"Someone hid me. I owe them my existence."*

### Backstory

You manifested quietly. Not in an explosion — in a whisper. A back alley in a settlement near the Vale border, at night, witnessed by a single person: a human who could have called for guards but instead knelt down, wrapped a cloak around your glowing shoulders, and said: "You need to be quiet. Come with me."

Your sympathizer — their name is Dara, and they are a specific NPC with a name and a history — hid you for nearly three weeks. They taught you language, movement, social norms. They fed you, explained the world, and slowly helped you understand what you are. They are the reason you're not a feral energy construct wandering the wilderness.

When the Vanguard started conducting door-to-door searches for "Severance anomalies," Dara gave you human clothing, supplies, and a route to Hollowford. You parted at the settlement gate. You don't know if they're safe.

### Opening Sequence

The player's opening is a compressed montage of the three weeks with Dara (experienced as a 10-minute gameplay sequence), followed by the escape.

Tutorial covers:
- **Social learning:** Dara teaches you to speak (simplified dialogue choices — the player is learning to communicate). Establishes the dialogue system in a low-stakes, emotionally warm context.
- **Concealment:** Dara teaches you to hide your glow — wearing gloves, keeping your hood up, controlling your emotional state (glow intensifies with emotion). Introduces the social-stealth concept unique to this origin.
- **The search:** Vanguard soldiers knock on Dara's door. The player must hide (stealth tutorial) while Dara deflects questions. Tension without combat.
- **Combat:** During the escape from the settlement, a patrol spots you. First combat — framed as desperate and unplanned. You don't want to fight — you want to run. Phase Step taught here as an escape tool, not an offensive one.
- **Emotional beat:** The parting with Dara. They press the cloak into your hands, say "Find me when this is over," and push you through the gate. The player walks away. They don't look back (the camera locks forward — a deliberate creative choice). The Echo, which has been quiet during the Dara sequence, finally speaks: *"They... are good. Remember that. Good exists."*

### Starting Stats & Gear

**Stats:** +2 Empathy, +1 Perception

**Passive Abilities:**
- **Human Passing:** With concealing clothing equipped (hood, gloves), the player's Aetherborn nature is not immediately detected in casual social interactions. Guards don't flag you. Merchants serve you normally. NPCs treat you as a slightly odd human. However: prolonged interaction (quest dialogues, faction meetings, companion scenes), physical contact, or removing concealing clothing reveals the truth. Once an NPC knows, they know permanently — and their reaction depends on their disposition.
- **Empathic Resonance:** The weeks with Dara developed your capacity for emotional perception. In dialogue, you receive subtle cues about NPC emotional states (a faint glow around their portrait indicating fear, trust, deception, or genuine warmth). This is not mind-reading — it's highly developed empathy expressed through the Aetherborn's lattice-sense. It provides information; it doesn't provide control.

**Starting Gear:**
- Human clothing (tunic, trousers, hooded cloak, gloves — designed to conceal Aetherborn features. Provides minimal armor but critical social function.)
- Short blade (a kitchen knife Dara gave you. Functional as a starting weapon. Sentimental value.)
- Dara's pendant (a carved wooden token. Non-mechanical. Showing it to specific NPCs in the border settlement region triggers recognition — "You know Dara?" — and opens unique dialogue paths.)
- Small coin purse (Dara's savings. Not much, but more than a Fracture Core starts with.)
- A handwritten note from Dara with basic directions to Hollowford and the phrase "Be careful. Be kind. Come back." (Readable in inventory. No mechanical function. Emotional anchor.)

### Social Modifiers

**Positive Reactions:**
- NPCs who don't know you're Aetherborn treat you normally — a rare and valuable experience for this race. The Human Passing ability means the Settlement Emergent has the most socially comfortable early game of any Aetherborn origin.
- When your nature IS revealed to sympathetic NPCs, some respond with heightened respect — you've been navigating their world successfully, which proves you're not the mindless anomaly the propaganda describes.
- Dara, if found alive, becomes a permanent NPC ally with unique quest and dialogue content.

**Negative Reactions:**
- NPCs who discover your concealment feel betrayed. The trust penalty for being "found out" is sharper than the trust penalty for being openly Aetherborn. Some NPCs who would have been neutral or sympathetic become hostile because they feel deceived.
- Other Aetherborn (particularly Fracture Cores) may view you as hiding what you are — a form of self-denial that they find offensive or cowardly.
- If Dara is threatened or harmed because of their association with you (a possible quest outcome), the guilt and social fallout become a recurring narrative thread.

**Unique Dialogue Examples:**
- "Someone taught me that your world is worth understanding. I'm still deciding if they were right." (Measured perspective)
- "You see a monster. I see someone who hasn't asked my name yet." (Challenging prejudice, quietly)
- "I know how to pretend to be one of you. I'm choosing not to right now." (Agency in identity)
- "Dara told me that fear makes people cruel but not evil. I'm testing that theory." (Referencing the sympathizer)

### Exclusive Side Quests

**"The Debt"**
Dara is alive — but the Vanguard has flagged them as a Severance-anomaly sympathizer. They're under surveillance, their business is being shut down, and they're one bad day from arrest. The player must intervene — through political leverage (call in faction favors), subterfuge (forge documents clearing Dara's name), direct action (extract Dara from the settlement), or negotiation (convince the local Vanguard commander that Dara's knowledge of Aetherborn is a military asset, not a crime). Each approach has different consequences. Success brings Dara to Hollowford. Failure has permanent narrative consequences.

# SECTION 6: NPC REACTION FRAMEWORK

## 6.1 How NPC Reactions Work

NPC disposition toward the player is determined by a layered system:

**Layer 1 — Race Baseline:** Every NPC has a default disposition toward each race. Most humans are neutral-to-suspicious toward Vaelari and hostile-to-fearful toward Aetherborn. This is the starting point, not the ceiling.

**Layer 2 — Origin Recognition:** Specific NPCs recognize origin markers (accent, clothing, items, physical traits) and adjust. A noble recognizes a Vale Nobility human. A Vaelari elder recognizes a Driftless by their silvered hair and darkened veining. A Vanguard officer recognizes an Aetherborn by their glow.

**Layer 3 — Reputation:** The player's actions override baseline dispositions over time. High Vanguard reputation means Vanguard NPCs treat you well regardless of race. High Hollowford reputation means local merchants serve you regardless of species.

**Layer 4 — Individual NPC Personality:** Some NPCs are personally xenophobic regardless of your reputation. Some are personally sympathetic regardless of your race. These are fixed traits that create narrative texture.

## 6.2 Specific Reaction Protocols

**Guards/Military (Vanguard):**
- Human: Neutral. Standard processing. May hassle based on origin (Scavenger-type origins get more scrutiny).
- Vaelari: Suspicious. Additional questioning. May restrict movement in certain areas. Weapons checks.
- Aetherborn: Hostile alert. Detainment likely unless reputation is high or the player has Vanguard sponsorship. Weapons drawn. Backup called.

**Merchants:**
- Human: Normal service. Prices vary by origin and reputation.
- Vaelari: Most serve normally but with visible discomfort. 10-15% price markup at some vendors. A few refuse service entirely (marked in the world — the player learns which merchants are friendly).
- Aetherborn: Many refuse service. Those who don't charge 20-30% markup. Black market vendors serve everyone equally (premium prices, but equal-opportunity premium prices).

**Civilians (Hollowford residents, refugees, travelers):**
- Human: Varied. Depends on individual NPC personality, origin recognition, and current events.
- Vaelari: Curiosity, unease, or hostility depending on the NPC and the context. Children are often curious. Adults are often wary. Refugees from regions that suffered Vaelari-adjacent disasters are sometimes hostile.
- Aetherborn: Fear. Avoidance. Whispered conversations. Occasionally: wonder (a child reaches toward the glow; a scholar stares in fascination). The spectrum is weighted toward negative but not uniformly so.

**Faction Leaders:**
- All faction leaders react based on political calculation, not personal prejudice. Kael, Aelira, Varek, and Seraphine all engage with the player regardless of race — they want what you can do, not what you are. Their personal feelings may differ from their professional behavior, and that gap is explorable through companion relationship development.

# SECTION 8: APPEARANCE CUSTOMIZATION

## 8.1 Human Customization

**Face:** 20+ presets per ethnicity grouping (60+ total). Sliders for facial structure (jaw, cheekbones, nose), eye shape and color, mouth, brow shape, and age (wrinkles, weathering, skin damage).

**Body:** Height slider (1.6m–1.9m). Build presets (muscular, average, lean — affects silhouette slightly, no gameplay impact). Scar placement (face and body — each scar has an optional lore tooltip the player can write or select).

**Hair:** 30+ styles (practical, grounded — no anime spikes or fantasy excess). Full natural color spectrum plus gray for age. 15+ facial hair styles for masculine characters. Stubble growth option (facial hair grows slowly over in-game time if enabled).

**Markings:** Cultural tattoos (Vale patterns, Highland crests, frontier brands — optional). Placement: arms, neck, face. Each pattern has a brief lore description.

**Relic Mark:** Placement choice affects visibility. Chest — usually concealed by clothing, revealed dramatically during key story moments. Forearm — visible during gameplay, commented on by NPCs. Neck — always visible, hardest to conceal, most NPC reactions.

## 8.2 Vaelari Customization

**Skin Tone:** Pale spectrum with cool undertones — sliders for base tone (alabaster through dusky gray) and undertone warmth (cooler = more silver/blue, warmer = more olive/ash). Vein pattern intensity slider (subtle to pronounced — controls visibility of the marble-like veining on temples, wrists, and neck).

**Ears:** Length slider (moderate to pronounced point). Angle slider (swept back vs. upright).

**Eyes:** Color selection across the unusual spectrum (pale green, silver-green, amber, ice blue, violet — 20+ shades). Pupil elongation slider (barely perceptible to clearly non-human). Glow intensity slider (dim to visible).

**Hair:** 30+ styles (long braided, loose, tied back, shorter practical cuts — grounded, no fantasy excess). Color: silver-white, ash blonde, deep auburn, blue-black, faded green-gray, and other unusual-but-natural tones. Texture options (straight, gently waved, fine). Accessories: bone clasps, wood pins, leather ties (cultural, optional).

**Face:** 20+ presets with angular, high-cheekboned base structure. Sliders for jaw definition, cheekbone prominence, nose shape, brow angle. Age slider — not wrinkles, but a weathered-stone quality (subtle, affects the weight of the face rather than adding lines).

**Body:** Height slider (1.9m–2.2m). Build slider within the lean range (wiry to graceful — no bulky options, but variation within the ectomorphic frame).

**Origin Marks:**
- Newly Awakened: Faint silvery residue on skin from chrysalis fluid — like dried salt patterns on the shoulders and forearms. Subtle, catches light. Automatic at creation, fades under armor later.
- Voluntary Driftless: Premature silver in hair (severity slider), slightly dulled eye glow, more pronounced vein patterns (the body showing strain from severed connection). The Driftless looks visibly different from a healthy Vaelari — this is deliberate and narratively meaningful.

**Relic Mark:** Organic-flowing runic pattern (Firstborn glyphs with curving, natural-feeling line work rather than sharp geometry). Green-gold glow. Same placement options as Human.

## 8.3 Aetherborn Customization

**Skin Tone:** Pale spectrum with blue, lavender, or silver undertone. Translucency slider (more translucent = more visible internal veins; less = more solid appearance).

**Eyes:** Solid color — luminous blue, white, lavender, silver. No pupil. Glow intensity slider.

**Hair:** Ethereal types (flowing, floating, short-cropped, or long-trailing — all with zero-gravity behavior). Color: white, pale blue, lavender, silver exclusively.

**Arcane Features:**
- Vein pattern selection (circuit-like, organic-branching, or geometric — affects the visual pattern of glowing veins beneath the skin)
- Instability frequency slider (cosmetic — controls how often the visual "glitch" effect occurs. High frequency = looks more unstable; low = more composed. No mechanical effect.)
- Relic mark coverage (Aetherborn marks are larger — slider from palm-sized to torso-covering)

**Origin Marks:**
- Fracture Core: Mark is always large and prominent. Body has faint scorch patterns from the fracture event.
- Settlement Emergent: Mark is moderate, partially concealed by clothing choices. Appearance is deliberately more "human-passing" in default configuration.

# SECTION 9: EXPANSION-READY RACES (Brief Profiles)

These races are designed for future content. Their lore exists in the World Bible. Their mechanical identities are outlined here at a high level to ensure launch content doesn't contradict expansion plans.

## 9.1 Ironkin — The Hollowed Covenant

*"The forges are silent. We remain."*

**Concept:** Ritually-created humanoids forged by the Firstborn. Their bodies are infused with metal at the molecular level — denser, tougher, and more durable than any organic race. They were originally created as a labor force and later evolved into an independent culture organized around forge-temples and religious ritual.

**Mechanical Direction:** Tank, heavy warrior, crafter. Highest physical durability. Lowest mobility. Unique crafting system (Firstborn Forge mastery). Iron Will system (defensive power spike below 25% HP). Weakness to water (metal-infused bodies conduct and corrode), lattice resonance pain.

**Expansion Region:** Highwinter Expanse.

**Why Not at Launch:** The Ironkin's tanking identity overlaps with gear-based tanking available to Humans. Their crafting system requires the Loot & Itemization system to be fully built before it can be differentiated. And their homeland (Highwinter) is not adjacent to the Vale, making their presence in the launch region less organically justified.

## 9.2 Drakelings — The Ashen Exodus

*"You see that glow on the horizon? That's my home. Still burning."*

**Concept:** Reptilian humanoids native to the volcanic Crimson Flats. Fire-resistant, heat-generating, and culturally devastated — the Severance triggered a volcanic cascade that destroyed their homeland. They are refugees, carrying literal internal fire (a Heat Meter system) and the trauma of a destroyed civilization.

**Mechanical Direction:** Berserker, fire mage, AoE pressure. Heat Meter (builds through combat, enhances fire abilities, risks uncontrolled ignition if it maxes out). High DPS ceiling. Vulnerability to cold/water. Social hostility (humans fear fire-beings in a world already burning).

**Expansion Region:** Crimson Flats (reclamation/survival story) or Sunscar Coast (diaspora story).

**Why Not at Launch:** The Heat Meter is the most mechanically complex unique system and requires extensive balance testing against the combat framework. Drakelings' fire identity creates environmental interaction requirements (flammable objects, heat-sensitive NPCs, fire-immunity zones) that multiply content creation costs. Their homeland destruction is a powerful story, but it's self-contained enough to work as expansion content without losing impact.
