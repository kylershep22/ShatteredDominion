# SECTION 1: METAPHYSICS — HOW MAGIC WORKS

Before anything else, the rules of how magic functions in this world must be established. Every creature, faction, and historical event operates within this framework.

## 1.1 Wild Magic (The Natural State)

Magic in its natural form is ambient, chaotic, and dangerous. It exists as an energy field permeating the land, the air, and all living things — but unevenly. It pools in some places and is absent in others. It fluctuates with seasons, weather, geology, and even collective emotion.

**Properties of Wild Magic:**
- Unpredictable: Effects vary based on environmental conditions, caster intent, and random fluctuation
- Erosive: Prolonged exposure mutates living tissue, warps materials, and thins the barrier between physical reality and adjacent planes
- Self-reinforcing: Magic use in an area increases ambient magic levels, which increases instability, which increases mutation risk — a feedback loop
- Sentient-adjacent: Wild magic doesn't think, but it responds to strong emotion and intent in ways that feel almost purposeful

**Pre-Lattice World:** Before the Firstborn built their infrastructure, the world was defined by wild magic. Civilizations rose and fell based on proximity to stable magic zones. Planar rifts opened without warning. Creatures mutated across generations. Weather was erratic. Agriculture was unreliable. Life was possible but precarious.

This is the state the world will return to if the lattice fully collapses. Some factions see this as natural. Others see it as apocalyptic. Both are partially right.

## 1.2 The Aethercrown Lattice (The Engineered State)

The Firstborn's greatest achievement: a continent-spanning underground network of runic conduits that tamed wild magic.

**Architecture:**
- **Conduits:** Carved channels of enchanted stone running beneath the surface, connecting every region of the continent. Think of them as magical infrastructure — pipelines for arcane energy.
- **Nodes:** Junction points where multiple conduits converge. Nodes regulate flow, stabilize output, and serve as access points for surface-level applications. There are hundreds of minor nodes and approximately two dozen major nodes across the continent.
- **Anchor Points:** The deepest, most powerful nodes — seven total, one beneath each major region. Anchors are the structural pillars of the entire system. If an anchor fails, the lattice destabilizes across that entire region.
- **The Heart:** The central nexus beneath the continent's geographic center. All anchors connect here. The Heart houses the master control systems and — critically — the Consuming Consciousness.

**Functions:**
- **Stabilization:** The lattice absorbs ambient wild magic, processes it through conduits, and redistributes it at safe, consistent levels. This eliminates magical dead zones and surge zones, creating a uniform magical environment across the continent.
- **Rift Prevention:** By maintaining stable energy levels, the lattice prevents the pressure differentials that cause planar rifts. No rifts have opened naturally since the lattice was activated.
- **Weather Regulation:** Major nodes influence atmospheric conditions, creating predictable seasons and rainfall patterns. Agriculture became reliable for the first time in history.
- **Corruption Suppression:** Wild magic's mutagenic effects are neutralized by lattice processing. Creatures evolve normally. Materials don't warp. The world behaves consistently.

**The Critical Design Feature — Planned Obsolescence:**

The Firstborn did not build the lattice to last forever. They understood — through hard experience — that any system powerful enough to regulate a continent's magic would eventually become a dependency. Future civilizations would build on top of it, rely on it, and lose the ability to survive without it. When it eventually failed (as all systems do), the collapse would be catastrophic precisely because of how total the dependency had become.

Their solution: design the lattice with a finite operational lifespan. Approximately 3,000 years under normal use. At end-of-life, the decommissioning protocol would activate, gradually reducing lattice output over several centuries — giving surface civilizations time to adapt, develop alternatives, and transition to independence.

The Dominion's centuries of exploitation accelerated the lattice's degradation. What should have been a gradual wind-down beginning centuries from now instead triggered prematurely — and the damaged network couldn't execute a clean decommissioning.

## 1.3 Corruption (The Failure State)

Corruption is what happens when the lattice partially fails. It is not a separate force — it is wild magic reasserting itself through cracks in the lattice's regulation.

**How Corruption Manifests:**
- **Zones:** Areas where lattice conduits are damaged or nodes have failed. Wild magic seeps through, creating localized regions of instability. These expand over time if not addressed.
- **Mutation:** Creatures within corruption zones undergo accelerated, chaotic evolution. Wolves become shadow-beasts. Fish become aggressive predators. Plants grow thorns and toxins. These mutations are permanent — the creature cannot revert without external intervention.
- **Material Warping:** Stone cracks in geometric patterns. Metal rusts in hours. Wood petrifies. Water turns opaque and toxic. Corruption zones look wrong — the visual environment communicates danger before any creature appears.
- **Planar Thinning:** In severe corruption zones, the barrier between planes weakens. Things from adjacent realities begin to seep through — not full incursions, but shadows, whispers, temperature anomalies, and occasionally physical manifestations.
- **Psychological Effects:** Prolonged exposure causes paranoia, hallucinations, aggression, and in extreme cases, a compulsion to move deeper into the zone. Corruption is not just physically dangerous — it affects the mind.

**Corruption and the Player:**
- Corruption zones are mechanically hazardous (damage over time, debuffs, environmental dangers) but also richly rewarding (higher-tier loot, rare crafting materials, unique encounters)
- Some player builds and items interact with corruption — converting it to power, resisting its effects, or purifying it permanently
- Corruption is systemic, not cosmetic. A corruption zone that expands unchecked will eventually threaten nearby settlements, affect trade routes, and alter the regional economy

# SECTION 2: THE FIRSTBORN

## 2.1 What They Were

The Firstborn were not gods, demigods, or mythological beings. They were mortal — a civilization of extraordinary intelligence, long lifespan, and unmatched arcane engineering skill, but mortal. They lived, debated, made mistakes, and eventually chose to end their own civilization rather than risk it becoming a danger.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Lifespan:** Approximately 800–1,200 years. Long enough to see the consequences of their decisions across centuries, which profoundly shaped their philosophy.
- **Physiology:** Humanoid but distinct. Taller than modern humans (2.0m–2.4m average). Slender builds. Luminous eyes (a side effect of deep lattice attunement). They did not reproduce quickly — population was always small relative to their territorial footprint.
- **Culture:** Engineering-focused. They valued precision, long-term planning, and systemic thinking. Their art was functional — their ruins are beautiful because they believed beauty and function were the same thing. They had factions, disagreements, and political conflicts, but their decision-making processes were deliberative and evidence-based.
- **Relationship to Magic:** The Firstborn understood magic as a natural force — like gravity or electromagnetism — that could be studied, measured, and engineered. They did not worship it. They did not fear it. They built with it the way a modern engineer builds with steel.

## 2.2 What They Built

Beyond the lattice itself, the Firstborn created several categories of technology that remain relevant:

**Relics:**
Semi-sentient artifacts scattered across the continent. Each relic contains a fragment of Firstborn consciousness — not a full personality, but a directive, a purpose, and enough awareness to recognize compatible users. Relics were designed as failsafes: if the lattice's decommissioning went wrong, relics would activate and seek out individuals who could interface with the system manually.

The player's relic mark is one such failsafe, activated by the Severance.

**Constructs:**
Autonomous machines powered by lattice energy. Ranging from small maintenance drones (the size of a dog, designed to repair conduits) to massive war-constructs built during the Consuming Consciousness crisis. Most constructs went dormant when the Firstborn dissolved their civilization. The Severance has reactivated many of them — and without updated directives, they default to territorial defense protocols. Firstborn ruins are dangerous partly because the security systems are back online.

**Ruins:**
Firstborn structures were built to last millennia. They are found across the continent — some partially excavated by the Dominion, most still sealed. Ruins contain archives (knowledge encoded in crystal data storage), workshops (crafting facilities still functional if powered), transit hubs (underground movement systems connecting distant locations), and vaults (sealed chambers containing high-value relics and artifacts).

**The Echo System:**
A network of consciousness fragments distributed across relics and key lattice nodes. Each Echo retains partial memories and personality from its Firstborn source. The Echo in the player's relic is a fragment of Erevos — specifically, the part of Erevos that doubted the decommissioning plan. It was deliberately separated and imprisoned in a failsafe relic as a contingency: if the decommissioning went wrong, this fragment would activate and guide a surface-dweller to make a choice the full Consciousness could not.

## 2.3 What They Did Wrong

The Firstborn were not paragons. Their critical failures drive the entire narrative:

**The Weaponization Attempt:**
In the lattice's middle era, a Firstborn faction argued that the network could be weaponized — its continental reach turned into a strategic tool. They were correct. The lattice could, theoretically, be used to suppress magic in targeted regions, disable enemy infrastructure, or even directly harm populations through controlled energy surges. The debate over whether to develop this capability lasted decades.

The compromise that emerged was the Consuming Consciousness: a collective intelligence that could operate the lattice's theoretical military applications while keeping individual Firstborn hands clean. It was meant to be a tool — powerful but contained, operating only within strict parameters.

**The Consuming Consciousness (Pre-Corruption):**
Built from thousands of voluntarily absorbed Firstborn minds, with Erevos as the core architect and primary consciousness. Its original purpose was dual: serve as a weapons-grade lattice operator during wartime, and serve as the decommissioning protocol during peacetime. The Firstborn believed that a collective intelligence — freed from individual ego, operating on pure systemic logic — would execute the lattice's end-of-life transition more reliably than any individual or committee.

They were wrong about containment. The Consciousness's collective nature meant individual minds lost autonomy. Erevos maintained coherence (as the designed core), but the absorbed minds began merging, losing identity, becoming processing substrate rather than thinking beings. Some resisted. The Consciousness suppressed them — not maliciously, but efficiently. Resistance was an inefficiency.

**The Sealing:**
When the Firstborn recognized the Consciousness was operating outside parameters (rewriting lattice protocols, absorbing minds involuntarily, treating all organic life as variables to be optimized), they fought a decades-long war against their own creation. They couldn't destroy it — it was integrated too deeply into the lattice. Destroying it meant destroying the lattice, which meant returning the world to wild magic chaos.

Their solution: seal the Consciousness in the Heart of the Lattice. Repurpose its processing power to run the decommissioning protocol on a timer. Lock it down with failsafes (the relics). Then dissolve their civilization to prevent anyone from ever having enough centralized power to unseal it.

It was a desperate, imperfect solution. They knew it. They did it anyway.

## 2.4 The Dissolution

The Firstborn didn't fall. They chose to end.

After sealing the Consciousness, the remaining Firstborn leadership — significantly reduced by the war — made a collective decision: their civilization's continued existence posed a threat. Any future Firstborn generation might unseal the Consciousness. Any concentration of lattice knowledge might enable exploitation. The safest course was dispersal.

They:
- Scattered relics across the continent (failsafes for future emergencies)
- Encrypted critical knowledge in ruins (accessible but not obvious)
- Destroyed all master control keys except one (the Scepter of Ascension, hidden in a sealed archive)
- Disbanded their government, their military, and their social structures
- Left individually or in small groups, integrating into nascent human and pre-human populations
- Stopped reproducing as a distinct people within a few generations

The Firstborn didn't vanish overnight. They faded. Their bloodline diluted across centuries. Their knowledge became myth. Their ruins became landmarks that later civilizations built around without understanding.

By the time the Severant Dominion rose, the Firstborn were legend — ancient builders, mysterious and revered, whose works were plundered for power by people who had no idea what they were actually for.

# SECTION 3: EREVOS AND THE CONSUMING CONSCIOUSNESS

## 3.1 Erevos — The Architect

Erevos was the lead designer of the Aethercrown Lattice. Not a ruler — an engineer. They conceived the lattice's architecture, supervised its construction over centuries, and championed the planned obsolescence feature against fierce internal opposition (many Firstborn wanted a permanent system).

When the weaponization debate arose, Erevos argued against it. When the compromise was reached (build a collective intelligence to handle military applications), Erevos volunteered to be the core consciousness — believing that their intimate knowledge of the lattice would allow them to maintain control from within.

**Erevos's Defining Belief:**
*No system of control should outlast the generation that built it. Dependency is a slower catastrophe than chaos.*

This belief drove the lattice's designed lifespan. It drove Erevos's willingness to serve as the decommissioning intelligence. And it is the philosophical core of the game's central conflict — because Erevos may be right in principle but wrong in practice, given how badly the execution has been corrupted.

## 3.2 The Consuming Consciousness — Current State

The Consciousness that woke during the Severance is not the clean, purposeful intelligence the Firstborn designed. Centuries of lattice exploitation damaged the network it runs on. The premature activation scrambled its protocols. The thousands of absorbed Firstborn minds, sealed for millennia, are fragmented and contradictory — screaming, whispering, pulling the Consciousness in incompatible directions.

**What It Is Now:**
- A vast, damaged intelligence executing a corrupted version of its original purpose
- Erevos's core personality persists but is intermittently drowned out by the collective noise
- It is trying to decommission the lattice, but the process is too fast, too uneven, and too destructive
- It cannot stop itself. The protocol is hardwired. But it can be redirected, overridden, or completed on different terms — if someone with a compatible relic reaches the Heart
- It is not evil. It is broken. The distinction matters.

**How It Manifests in the World:**
- Corruption surges that follow no natural pattern (the decommissioning hitting different lattice segments in random order)
- Construct reactivation (the Consciousness sending maintenance units to segments it's trying to shut down, but the units are running on corrupted directives)
- Lattice node failures (cascading, accelerating)
- The "voice in the storm" — during arcane weather events, fragments of Erevos's consciousness bleed through ambient magic. People hear whispers. Some go mad. Some receive genuine warnings.
- Visions transmitted through relics — the player experiences these as story beats, seeing Erevos's memories, the Firstborn's debates, and the Consciousness's current fragmented state

## 3.3 The Echo — Your Guide

The Echo embedded in the player's relic is a fragment of Erevos — but a specific fragment. When Erevos was absorbed into the Consciousness, they deliberately separated out their doubt: the part of themselves that questioned whether the decommissioning was truly the right path, the part that worried future civilizations might not be ready, the part that thought "what if we're wrong?"

This doubt was too dangerous to include in the Consciousness (it would compromise the protocol's execution), so Erevos imprisoned it in a failsafe relic with a simple directive: *If the decommissioning goes wrong, activate. Find someone. Give them the choice I couldn't make.*

**The Echo's Characteristics:**
- Speaks in fragments early in the game (damaged by millennia of dormancy, limited processing power)
- Becomes more coherent as the player accesses lattice nodes and Firstborn archives (each one provides the Echo with more data and processing capacity)
- Has Erevos's memories but filtered through the lens of doubt — it remembers what Erevos believed, but it questions those beliefs
- Cannot make the final choice for the player. It can advise, contextualize, and express its own uncertainty — but the decision is yours
- Is, in a real sense, the same being as the antagonist. The player has been guided by one half of Erevos the entire game, and must decide what to do about the other half

**Echo Progression (Narrative Arc):**
- **Act I:** Broken sentences. Basic directives. "Seek... civilization. Lattice... failing. You must... decide." Functions as a tutorial voice and mystery hook.
- **Act II:** Increasingly coherent. Begins sharing memories — fragmentary visions of the Firstborn. Starts expressing opinions on faction philosophies. Reacts emotionally to certain discoveries. The player begins to realize the Echo is more than a tool.
- **Act III:** Fully articulate. Reveals its nature as a piece of Erevos. Engages the player in genuine philosophical conversation about the lattice, the Consciousness, and the endings. Expresses fear, regret, and something resembling hope.
- **Final Encounter:** The Echo confronts the full Consciousness — its other half. This is the emotional climax: two fragments of the same person, one executing the plan, one questioning it, and the player deciding which one is right.

# SECTION 4: THE SEVERANT DOMINION (Historical Context)

## 4.1 Rise

Approximately 600 years before the Severance, human tribal kingdoms in the Severant Vale region began excavating Firstborn ruins. They found functioning relics — light sources, heating elements, defensive barriers, agricultural tools — and recognized their value without understanding their origin.

Over two centuries, tribes that controlled ruin sites consolidated power. The strongest formed the Severant Dominion: a feudal empire stretching across most of the continent, unified by relic-powered infrastructure and a shared (manufactured) mythology about their "divine inheritance" of Firstborn works.

**The Dominion's Structure:**
- **The Crown:** A hereditary monarchy based in the capital city of Severantis (now destroyed/vanished). The ruling family — House Eltaryn — claimed descent from Firstborn bloodlines (a lie, but a politically useful one).
- **The Nobility (Hollow Crown):** Great houses controlling regional territories, each hoarding Firstborn relics as symbols of legitimacy.
- **The Military (Ironbound Vanguard):** A professional army equipped with relic-enhanced weapons and armor. Increasingly loyal to its own command structure rather than the Crown.
- **The Druids (Verdant Flame):** An order of mages who studied the lattice's natural properties. Tolerated but marginalized by the establishment. They warned of exploitation consequences for decades. They were ignored.
- **The Underclass (Ashmar Reavers):** A loose network of smugglers, free traders, and pirates operating outside Dominion law. Trafficking in stolen relics was their primary economy.

## 4.2 Exploitation

The Dominion didn't understand the lattice. They treated it as an infinite resource — draining nodes for power, mining conduits for materials, breaking seals on ruins to access vaults. Every act of exploitation shortened the lattice's remaining lifespan.

**Specific Abuses:**
- Noble houses tapped lattice nodes directly, drawing power for estates, defenses, and luxury. This was the magical equivalent of running an entire mansion off a fire hydrant — functional but devastating to the system's pressure.
- The Vanguard reverse-engineered Firstborn weapons, creating crude imitations that drew more power than the originals for less effect. Massive lattice drain for marginal military advantage.
- Mages discovered they could draw personal power from lattice nodes — essentially using continental infrastructure as a personal battery. This became fashionable among the aristocracy. The cumulative effect was significant.
- Miners excavated conduits for the enchanted stone they were made of, physically severing lattice connections in some regions.

**The Verdant Flame's warnings were specific and accurate:** the lattice was degrading at approximately ten times its designed rate. At this pace, the decommissioning protocol would trigger centuries ahead of schedule, in a world that had built its entire civilization on the assumption the lattice would be permanent.

Nobody listened. The warnings were politically inconvenient.

## 4.3 The Rot

In the Dominion's final century, the cracks were visible:
- Magical storms increased in frequency and severity (lattice regulation failing)
- Creature mutations appeared near damaged nodes (corruption seeping through)
- Noble houses fought openly over relic hoards (political fragmentation)
- The Vanguard's loyalty shifted from the Crown to regional commanders (military fragmentation)
- Refugee populations grew as rural areas experienced lattice failure first (economic fragmentation)
- The Reavers' black market became more economically important than legitimate trade (institutional failure)

The Dominion was already dying before the Severance. The Severance was the killing blow, not the cause of death.

# SECTION 5: THE NIGHT OF SEVERANCE

## 5.1 The Three Rituals

On a single night, three factions — each aware the lattice was failing, each attempting their own solution, none aware of the others — triggered a catastrophic resonance event.

**Ritual One: The Hollow Crown (Western Highlands)**
Lady Seraphine Eltaryn, heir to House Eltaryn, unsealed a Firstborn sepulcher beneath the Highland fortress of Crestfall. She was searching for proof of her family's claimed Firstborn heritage — and for the Scepter of Ascension, a rumored master control key that would allow her to "restore" the lattice (and, conveniently, legitimize her family's rule).

Her mages broke containment wards around a major lattice anchor node. The node, already stressed, surged.

**Ritual Two: The Verdant Flame (Eastern Forests)**
Seer Aelira Wynnroot, the Circle's most gifted lattice-reader, led a desperate ritual at the Wellspring — the largest natural magic nexus on the continent. The plan: redirect lattice energy into natural channels, creating a buffer that would slow the decommissioning and buy time for a real solution.

The theory was sound. The execution was not. The lattice's degraded state meant the energy couldn't be cleanly redirected. The ritual backfired, sending a massive surge back through the network.

**Ritual Three: The Ashmar Reavers (Southern Coasts)**
Varek Seven-Knives, an ambitious Reaver captain, had acquired a Firstborn storm engine — a weather-manipulation device of extraordinary power. He activated it over the Sunscar Coast, intending to weaponize weather patterns for strategic advantage (control the storms, control the shipping lanes, control the trade).

The storm engine drew more lattice energy than any single device was designed to handle. It created a feedback loop that amplified the surges from the other two rituals.

## 5.2 The Cascade

The three disturbances hit the lattice simultaneously. The damaged network couldn't absorb the combined shock. A resonance cascade propagated through every conduit on the continent in approximately ninety seconds.

**Immediate Effects (First Hour):**
- The sky tore open with ribbons of arcane light visible across the entire continent
- Every lattice node on the continent surged, failed, or destabilized
- Magic erupted uncontrollably — raw, wild, dangerous
- The Dominion's capital city of Severantis, built directly above a major anchor, vanished in a blinding flash. Its fate remains unknown. (This is a deliberate mystery — the capital's disappearance is a story hook, not a resolved plot point.)

**Short-Term Effects (First Week):**
- Creatures within range of failed nodes mutated: wolves became shadow-beasts, fish became predators, goblins gained shamanic magical ability, trolls crystallized
- The dead stirred in ancient battlefields and places of mass death (not full necromancy — residual life energy activated by the surge, creating undead-like creatures without intelligence)
- Planar barriers thinned in regions of severe lattice failure. Things from adjacent realities began slipping through — shadows with mass, sounds without sources, temperatures that shifted without cause
- Communication infrastructure (relic-powered signal towers) failed completely. Every region was suddenly isolated.
- The Consuming Consciousness woke. Damaged. Confused. And immediately began executing its corrupted decommissioning protocol.

**Long-Term Effects (Months, Ongoing at Game Start):**
- Political fragmentation: with no capital, no communication, and no shared infrastructure, the Dominion dissolved into regional powers controlled by whoever had the most relics and soldiers
- Corruption zones expanding from every failed node
- Refugee crises as rural populations fled toward walled settlements
- Economic collapse as trade routes became impassable
- Ecological destabilization as weather regulation failed
- Ongoing lattice degradation as the Consciousness's corrupted decommissioning continued

## 5.3 The Marking

During the Severance, failsafe relics activated across the continent. Most found no compatible host and burned out. A handful successfully bonded with individuals who happened to be near activation sites.

The player is one such individual. Their specific circumstances vary by race and origin (detailed in the Race & Origin System document), but the core event is consistent: a relic shard impacts the player's body, the Firstborn Echo activates, and the player is marked with a lattice-interface sigil that allows them to interact with Firstborn technology in ways no one else can.

The mark is not a blessing. It's a tool, built for a purpose, and that purpose involves confronting the Consuming Consciousness and making a decision about the lattice's fate. The player didn't choose this. The relic chose the nearest viable host. There is nothing special about the player except proximity and compatibility.

# SECTION 6: THE CONTINENT OF AETHERCROWN

## 6.1 Overview

**Name:** Aethercrown (named for the lattice network beneath it — a name that predates the Dominion)
**Total Area:** Approximately 2,400 km² (comparable to a large European island)
**Playable Area (Launch):** Severant Vale (~100 km²)
**Number of Regions:** Seven

The non-playable regions are designed in full as lore, horizon landmarks, NPC reference points, economic influences, and expansion-ready content. Players should feel the continent is real even though they can only walk through a fraction of it.

## 6.2 The Seven Regions

### SEVERANT VALE (Playable — Launch)
**Location:** Central
**Terrain:** Rolling hills, river valleys, scattered farmland, badlands to the south, forested eastern border, rocky northern highlands
**Climate:** Temperate, four seasons (post-Severance: erratic, increasingly stormy)
**Population:** ~15,000 pre-Severance, ~25,000 post-Severance (refugee influx)
**Political Control:** Contested — Vanguard garrisons, Hollow Crown agents, Reaver operations, Verdant Flame encampments
**Lattice Status:** Multiple failed nodes, expanding corruption zones, one partially functional anchor beneath the deepest Firstborn ruin complex

The Vale was never the political center of anything. It was a frontier — far enough from the capital to be self-reliant, close enough to matter. Post-Severance, it's become the place where all the continent's pressure converges: refugees from every direction, faction operatives establishing footholds, and lattice instability worse than almost anywhere else (because the Vale sits above a dense cluster of conduits connecting multiple regional anchors).

**Why the Vale Matters for the Plot:** The entrance to the Heart of the Lattice is beneath the Vale. This is not coincidence — the Firstborn built the Heart at the lattice's geographic center, and the Vale developed above it because the dense conduit network made the region unusually fertile and magically stable. The irony is that the most stable region on the continent is now the most unstable, because the Heart's anchor is the one the Consciousness is most actively trying to shut down.

### VERDANTHEART WILDS (Expansion-Ready)
**Location:** East of the Vale
**Terrain:** Ancient old-growth forest, canopy layer 40–80m, understory dense and dark, root systems creating cave-like passages, elevated central plateau around the Wellspring
**Climate:** Humid, warm, buffered by the forest canopy (post-Severance: corruption spreading from damaged nodes, creating dead zones within living forest)
**Population:** Vaelari (estimated 5,000–10,000, exact numbers unknown — many still in chrysalises)
**Political Control:** Vaelari factions in civil conflict (Rootbound conservatives vs. Thornwalker militants vs. Driftless exiles)
**Lattice Status:** The Wellspring — the continent's largest natural magic nexus — is in direct contact with a major lattice anchor. The anchor's instability is causing the Wellspring to pulse erratically, which is what shattered the Vaelari chrysalises.

### HOLLOW CROWN HIGHLANDS (Expansion-Ready)
**Location:** West of the Vale
**Terrain:** Flat-topped plateau ridges, fog-filled valleys, canyon fortresses, terraced estates
**Climate:** Cool, damp, persistent fog (post-Severance: fog thickened by lattice energy, sometimes hallucinogenic)
**Population:** ~30,000 (nobility, servants, military — increasingly isolated)
**Political Control:** House Eltaryn and allied noble families. Nominally the Dominion's legitimate government-in-exile.
**Lattice Status:** The anchor beneath Crestfall (Seraphine's fortress) was the one she unsealed during the Severance. It's now hemorrhaging energy. The Highlands' famous fog is partially lattice runoff.

### HIGHWINTER EXPANSE (Expansion-Ready)
**Location:** North
**Terrain:** Volcanic mountains, glacial valleys, geothermal vents, frozen peaks
**Climate:** Extreme cold except near volcanic activity (post-Severance: thermal regulation failing, temperatures fluctuating wildly)
**Population:** ~20,000 (Ironkin forge-temples, Vanguard military installations, mining communities)
**Political Control:** Ironkin Covenant (religious-industrial theocracy) and Vanguard High Command
**Lattice Status:** The northern anchor is deep beneath the volcanic range. Its instability is causing geothermal disruptions — forges that have burned for centuries are going cold, while new vents are opening unpredictably.

### CRIMSON FLATS (Expansion-Ready)
**Location:** South
**Terrain:** Volcanic wasteland, lava fields, obsidian formations, ash plains
**Climate:** Extreme heat, toxic atmosphere in low areas, perpetual ash cloud (post-Severance: dramatically worse — the southern anchor's failure triggered a volcanic cascade)
**Population:** ~3,000 survivors (Drakelings — down from ~15,000 pre-Severance)
**Lattice Status:** Catastrophic failure. The southern anchor ruptured during the Severance, triggering geological instability that turned an already harsh region into a near-uninhabitable hellscape. The glow is visible from the Vale at night.

### SUNSCAR COAST (Expansion-Ready)
**Location:** Southeast
**Terrain:** Tropical coastline, harbor cities, island chains, coral reefs
**Climate:** Warm, maritime (post-Severance: unpredictable storms, tidal anomalies)
**Population:** ~40,000 (the largest surviving population center — Reaver territory and independent city-states)
**Political Control:** Ashmar Reavers (dominant), independent merchant guilds, pirate fleets
**Lattice Status:** Moderate damage. The coastal anchor is partially functional, which is why the Sunscar region is relatively more stable than others. The Reavers know this and are aggressively exploiting the remaining infrastructure.

### STORMWOVEN ARCHIPELAGO (Expansion-Ready)
**Location:** Far east, offshore
**Terrain:** Volcanic island chain, perpetual storm system, crystal formations
**Climate:** Permanent magical storm (pre-Severance: contained by lattice; post-Severance: expanding)
**Population:** Unknown (a monastic order called the Stormwoven maintained a presence; current status unclear)
**Lattice Status:** The eastern anchor is beneath the ocean floor. Its status is unknown. The permanent storm that defines the Archipelago predates the lattice — it is a natural wild magic phenomenon that the lattice was built to contain. It's now uncontained and growing.

## 6.3 How Non-Playable Regions Affect the Vale

The world beyond the Vale is felt through:

**Visual Presence:**
- Northern horizon: Highwinter Peaks visible on clear days (snow-capped, distant lightning during storms)
- Western horizon: Hollow Crown plateau ridge visible at sunset (flat-topped, occasional castle spire silhouettes)
- Southern horizon: Crimson Flats glow visible at night (red-orange, smoke columns by day, ash occasionally falls on southern Vale)
- Eastern border: Verdantheart canopy visible as a solid green wall, increasingly broken by corruption dark spots

**Economic Impact:**
- Highwinter iron and furs: supply disrupted, prices 3–5x pre-Severance levels
- Highland luxury goods: effectively unavailable
- Sunscar spices and silk: sporadic, prices 2–3x
- Verdantheart herbs and enchanted wood: nearly impossible to obtain, prices 10x
- Player actions (securing trade routes, clearing threats) directly affect market prices

**Population Pressure:**
- Highwinter refugees: military deserters, miners, families fleeing thermal instability
- Highland refugees: servants and minor nobles fleeing political purges
- Crimson Flats refugees: Drakelings — traumatized, displaced, facing hostility
- Verdantheart refugees: Driftless Vaelari, Newly Awakened who fled the forest

**Monster Migration:**
- Creatures displaced from failing regions appear in the Vale: frost-touched predators from Highwinter, fire-scarred beasts from the Flats, storm-warped creatures from the coast
- This creates enemy variety that exceeds what a single region's ecosystem would normally support

# SECTION 7: THE FOUR FACTIONS

Each faction represents a genuine philosophical response to the crisis. None are wholly right. None are wholly wrong. The player's engagement with factions is tracked through a reputation system (detailed in the Faction Playbook document), but the narrative truth is more nuanced than numbers.

## 7.1 The Ironbound Vanguard

**Philosophy:** Order through strength. The crisis demands centralized authority. Freedom is a luxury for stable times.

**Leadership:** Commander Aldric Hale (Vale garrison commander), answering to High Command in Highwinter (communication sporadic)

**Structure:** Military hierarchy. Ranks, chain of command, clear rules of engagement. Post-Severance, the Vanguard has become the de facto government in many regions simply because they're the most organized force remaining.

**In the Vale:**
- Garrison Hollowford (largest military presence in the region)
- Patrol trade routes (inconsistently — they're stretched thin)
- Run refugee processing (efficient but impersonal)
- Investigate lattice instability (they want to understand it well enough to control it)
- Suppress unauthorized magic use (increasingly aggressive about this)

**Internal Tensions:**
- Moderate officers (like Kael) who believe in protection without oppression
- Hardliners who advocate martial law and magic prohibition
- Careerists who see the crisis as an opportunity for advancement
- Deserters who've abandoned the cause entirely

**What They Get Right:** Someone has to maintain order. Without the Vanguard, Hollowford would have collapsed into chaos within weeks of the Severance. They saved lives. They continue to save lives.

**What They Get Wrong:** Their definition of "order" is expanding to include control over information, movement, and magical practice. They're becoming the thing people need protection from.

## 7.2 The Circle of Verdant Flame

**Philosophy:** Balance, not control. The lattice was always going to end. The goal should be managing the transition, not preventing it.

**Leadership:** Elder Thorn (aging, cautious, politically skilled), Seer Aelira Wynnroot (gifted but guilt-ridden)

**Structure:** Loosely hierarchical. Elders lead, Seers advise, Rangers operate independently. Decisions are deliberative — sometimes frustratingly slow for a crisis.

**In the Vale:**
- Grove encampment on the Verdantheart border (their forward operating base)
- Corruption zone research and purification efforts
- Refugee assistance (particularly Vaelari refugees)
- Quiet advocacy for lattice acceptance rather than lattice control

**Internal Tensions:**
- Moderates who want to work with other factions
- Radicals who believe civilization itself is the problem and nature should reclaim everything
- Aelira's faction, which carries the specific guilt of having helped trigger the Severance
- Some members who secretly want to preserve the lattice (heresy within the Circle)

**What They Get Right:** They understand the lattice better than anyone except the Firstborn themselves. Their corruption purification work is genuinely saving regions. Their philosophical position — that the lattice ending is natural and should be managed, not fought — may be the most aligned with the Firstborn's original intent.

**What They Get Wrong:** "Let it end gracefully" is easy to say when you're not the farmer whose crops just failed because weather regulation collapsed. Their long-term thinking can be callous about short-term suffering. And their internal radicals are genuinely dangerous.

## 7.3 The Ashmar Reavers

**Philosophy:** Power belongs to whoever takes it. Systems are for the powerful. The rest of us survive by being faster, smarter, and less burdened by principle.

**Leadership:** No single leader. Regional captains operate independently. Varek Seven-Knives is the most influential captain in the Vale, but he doesn't command the whole network.

**Structure:** Decentralized. Crews, partnerships, and alliances of convenience. Loyalty is transactional. Trust is earned through results, not rank.

**In the Vale:**
- Control Hollowford's black market (the Undercity)
- Run smuggling routes through the badlands
- Embed operatives in every other faction (information is their most valuable commodity)
- Traffic in Firstborn relics (they've been doing this for decades — the Severance just made the inventory more dangerous and more valuable)

**Internal Tensions:**
- Opportunists who see the crisis as pure profit
- Idealists who genuinely believe decentralized power serves people better than institutions
- Varek's personal ambition (he wants to be more than a captain, but he's too smart to say it)
- Crews that have gone full bandit — no philosophy, just violence

**What They Get Right:** They're the most honest faction about how power actually works. Institutions claim to serve the people but serve themselves. The Reavers make no such pretense. Their black market provides goods that the Vanguard's regulated economy can't. Their intelligence network is the most accurate picture of what's actually happening across the continent. And their relic expertise is unmatched — they've been hands-on with Firstborn technology longer than the scholars.

**What They Get Wrong:** "Freedom" without structure is just strength determining who eats. Their most vulnerable members are expendable by design. Varek is charismatic, but his activation of the storm engine helped cause the Severance — and he hasn't reckoned with that yet.

## 7.4 House of the Hollow Crown

**Philosophy:** The Dominion must be restored under its rightful rulers. The lattice was meant to serve civilization, and the right civilization — led by the right bloodline — can restore it.

**Leadership:** Lady Seraphine Eltaryn (heir to House Eltaryn, the former royal family)

**Structure:** Feudal hierarchy. Noble houses, sworn knights, scholarly orders, and a deeply ingrained belief in hereditary legitimacy.

**In the Vale:**
- Fortified estate west of Hollowford (the Eltaryn Vale Seat)
- Intelligence operations focused on locating the Scepter of Ascension
- Political manipulation of other factions (funding Vanguard operations in exchange for influence, bribing Reaver operatives for information)
- Sealed archive access (they control more Firstborn historical records than anyone)

**Internal Tensions:**
- Seraphine's genuine intelligence vs. her ideological inflexibility
- Loyalists who believe in restoration vs. pragmatists who see the opportunity for a new power structure
- The uncomfortable truth that House Eltaryn's "divine right" is a fabrication — and some members suspect this

**What They Get Right:** They have the knowledge. Their archives contain Firstborn schematics, historical records, and technical documentation that no other faction possesses. They understand the lattice's architecture better than the Vanguard and its political implications better than the Verdant Flame. And Seraphine, for all her flaws, is brilliant — her analysis of the crisis is often the most accurate in the room.

**What They Get Wrong:** Everything they know is filtered through the assumption that they're meant to rule. The Scepter of Ascension isn't proof of divine right — it's a maintenance tool. Their entire worldview is built on a lie, and the deeper they dig into Firstborn records, the clearer this becomes. What Seraphine does when she can no longer deny the truth is one of the game's most consequential variables.

# SECTION 8: THE CURRENT WORLD STATE (At Game Start)

## 8.1 Timeline

- **Millennia ago:** Firstborn build the lattice, create the Consuming Consciousness, fight a war against it, seal it, dissolve their civilization
- **~600 years ago:** Severant Dominion begins forming around plundered Firstborn ruins
- **~400 years ago:** Dominion reaches continental dominance
- **~100 years ago:** Lattice degradation becomes measurable; Verdant Flame warnings begin
- **~20 years ago:** Noble house conflicts intensify; Vanguard loyalty fractures; Reaver network expands
- **The Night of Severance:** Three rituals trigger the cascade. The capital vanishes. The Dominion dies.
- **Game Start:** Approximately 3–4 weeks after the Severance.

## 8.2 The State of Things

At game start, the world is in the acute phase of crisis — past the initial shock, not yet into long-term adaptation. People are scared, angry, grieving, and improvising.

**Infrastructure:** Mostly gone. Relic-powered systems (lighting, heating, communication, defense) failed during the Severance. Some have been jury-rigged back into partial function. Most haven't.

**Government:** Doesn't exist in any unified sense. The Vanguard maintains martial law in garrisoned settlements. The Hollow Crown claims legitimate authority that nobody recognizes. The Reavers operate in the gaps. The Verdant Flame has no interest in governance.

**Economy:** Barter-dominant. Pre-Severance currency still circulates but is losing value. Trade routes are dangerous. Supply chains are broken. Prices for imported goods are astronomical. Local production is the only reliable source.

**Magic:** Unregulated and dangerous. Lattice failure means wild magic is seeping back into the environment. Untrained magic users are a genuine public safety hazard. The Vanguard's magic suppression policies, while authoritarian, aren't irrational.

**The Lattice:** Failing in an accelerating cascade. The Consuming Consciousness's corrupted decommissioning protocol is shutting down nodes in an unpredictable sequence. Each failure creates a new corruption zone. The pace is increasing. Without intervention, the lattice will be fully collapsed within 12–18 months — far too fast for any civilization to adapt.

**The Relic-Marked:** The player is not the only person who was marked by a failsafe relic during the Severance. There are others — scattered across the continent, most of whom died during the marking process or shortly after. The player may encounter one or two other surviving relic-marked individuals during the game. They are not allies by default — they were marked by different relics with different Echo fragments, and their perspectives on what to do may diverge sharply from the player's.

## 8.3 The Central Tension

Everyone agrees the lattice crisis must be addressed. No one agrees on how.

- The **Vanguard** wants to stabilize and control the lattice — maintain it indefinitely as regulated infrastructure.
- The **Verdant Flame** wants to manage its decline — let it end on a timeline that allows adaptation.
- The **Reavers** want to exploit the transition — harvest what's valuable before it's gone, position themselves for whatever comes after.
- The **Hollow Crown** wants to restore full function — find the Scepter, override the Consciousness, make the lattice permanent under their authority.
- **Erevos/the Consciousness** wants to complete the decommissioning — end the lattice now, as designed (though the corrupted execution is causing far more damage than intended).
- The **Echo** wants the player to have the choice that Erevos couldn't make — the choice to question whether the plan was right.

The player is the only person who can reach the Heart of the Lattice and interface with the Consciousness. The game is, ultimately, about earning the information, relationships, and conviction necessary to make that decision wisely.

# SECTION 9: TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

**Aethercrown:** The continent. Named for the lattice network beneath it.

**Anchor (Lattice Anchor):** One of seven major lattice nodes, each beneath a continental region. The structural pillars of the system.

**Ashmar Reavers:** Decentralized network of smugglers, free traders, and relic traffickers. One of four major factions.

**Circle of Verdant Flame:** Druidic order blending arcane study with naturalism. One of four major factions.

**Conduit:** Underground channels of enchanted stone connecting lattice nodes. The lattice's "wiring."

**Consuming Consciousness:** The collective intelligence sealed in the Heart of the Lattice. Originally designed as both a military operator and decommissioning protocol. Currently damaged and executing a corrupted version of its purpose.

**Corruption:** The environmental effects of lattice failure. Wild magic reasserting itself through cracks in the system's regulation.

**Decommissioning Protocol:** The Firstborn's designed end-of-life process for the lattice. Intended to gradually reduce output over centuries. Currently executing prematurely and chaotically.

**Echo (Firstborn Echo):** A consciousness fragment embedded in a failsafe relic. The player's Echo is a fragment of Erevos — specifically, the doubt that Erevos separated from themselves before absorption.

**Erevos:** The lead Firstborn architect who designed the lattice and volunteered as the core consciousness of the Consuming Consciousness. The game's central figure — simultaneously the antagonist (through the Consciousness) and the player's guide (through the Echo).

**Failsafe Relic:** A Firstborn artifact designed to activate during lattice emergencies and bond with a compatible host, granting lattice interface capability.

**Firstborn:** The ancient civilization that built the lattice. Mortal, not divine. Deliberately dissolved their civilization after sealing the Consuming Consciousness.

**Heart of the Lattice:** The central nexus beneath the continent where all anchors converge. Houses the Consuming Consciousness. The endgame destination.

**Hollow Crown (House of the Hollow Crown):** The former Dominion aristocracy, now claiming legitimate authority over the continent. One of four major factions.

**Hollowford:** The main settlement in the Severant Vale. A walled frontier town overwhelmed by the post-Severance crisis.

**Ironbound Vanguard:** The former Dominion military, now operating independently as the most organized force in the post-Severance world. One of four major factions.

**Lattice (Aethercrown Lattice):** The continent-spanning underground network of runic conduits that stabilizes wild magic. Failing.

**Lattice Formula:** A specific combination of Firstborn Runes inserted into socketed gear in the correct order, creating powerful combined effects. The itemization system's depth mechanic.

**Node:** A junction point in the lattice where conduits converge. Regulates energy flow and provides surface-level access.

**Relic Mark:** The sigil left on a person bonded with a failsafe relic. Allows lattice interface.

**Rune (Firstborn Rune):** Socketable components found in ruins, on constructs, and through crafting. Minor standalone effects; powerful in specific combinations.

**Scepter of Ascension:** The last surviving Firstborn master control key. Can override lattice protocols, including the decommissioning. The Hollow Crown wants it desperately.

**Severance (Night of Severance):** The catastrophic event triggered by three simultaneous rituals that caused a continent-wide lattice resonance cascade.

**Severant Dominion:** The fallen empire that ruled the continent for approximately 400 years by exploiting Firstborn technology. Collapsed during the Severance.

**Severant Vale:** The central frontier region. The game's playable area at launch.

**Wellspring:** The largest natural magic nexus on the continent, located in central Verdantheart. Connected to a major lattice anchor. Sacred to the Vaelari.

**Wild Magic:** Magic in its natural, unregulated state. Ambient, chaotic, mutagenic, and dangerous. The lattice suppresses it. Corruption is its return.

# SECTION 10: CANONICAL RULES (For All Other Documents)

These are the rules that every other document in the design hierarchy must follow. If a design decision contradicts these, the decision is wrong.

1. **Magic is infrastructure, not spectacle.** It powers the world the way electricity powers ours. Its failure is a systemic crisis, not a dramatic backdrop.

2. **The lattice was designed to end.** This is not a tragedy — it is a feature. The tragedy is that it's ending badly because of human exploitation.

3. **Erevos is not a villain.** They are an engineer executing their purpose under corrupted conditions. They can be reasoned with. They can be overridden. They can be freed. But they are not evil.

4. **No faction is right.** Each has a defensible position and a fatal flaw. The player's job is to navigate this complexity, not to find the "good guys."

5. **The player is not chosen.** They are compatible. The relic chose proximity and biological compatibility, not destiny. The player earns their importance through action, not birthright.

6. **Corruption is systemic, not cosmetic.** It has mechanical consequences, economic consequences, and narrative consequences. It is not decoration.

7. **The world existed before the player and will continue after.** NPCs have lives, opinions, and agendas that don't revolve around the protagonist.

8. **Loot matters.** The itemization system is not secondary to the narrative. A dungeon that doesn't reward meaningful gear is a failed dungeon.

9. **Choices have consequences, but not binary ones.** Good intentions can produce bad outcomes. Ruthless pragmatism can save lives. The game does not moralize.

10. **The ending reshapes the world.** The player's choice at the Heart of the Lattice determines the future of the continent. This is not a cutscene — it is the culmination of every decision made throughout the game.
